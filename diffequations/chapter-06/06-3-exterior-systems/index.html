Section 6.3: Exterior Differential Systems & Cartan-K√§hler

Differential ideals, integral manifolds

Section 6.3: Exterior Differential Systems & Cartan-K√§hler

Introduction

The reformulation of partial differential equations into the language of jet bundles, as established in the preceding sections, allows us to detach the concept of a solution from specific coordinate representations. However, to treat existence and uniqueness systematically‚Äîparticularly for overdetermined systems where standard analytic tools fail‚Äîwe require a coordinate-free algebraic framework. This framework is the theory of Exterior Differential Systems (EDS).

In this section, we replace the differential equation with a differential ideal in the exterior algebra. A solution is no longer a function, but an integral manifold of this ideal. This geometric shift allows us to utilize the powerful Cartan-K√§hler theorem, which serves as a broad generalization of the Cauchy-Kovalevskaya theorem for intrinsic geometries.

Mathematical Content

Differential Ideals and Integral Manifolds

The fundamental object of study in this framework is not a system of equations $F(x, u, p) = 0$, but a collection of differential forms that must vanish on any valid solution.

Definition 6.3.1 (Differential Ideal). Let $M$ be a smooth manifold. A differential ideal $\mathcal{I}$ is an algebraic ideal of the graded algebra of differential forms $\Omega^*(M)$ that satisfies two conditions:

  1. Algebraic Closure: If $\alpha \in \mathcal{I}$ and $\beta \in \Omega^*(M)$, then $\alpha \wedge \beta \in \mathcal{I}$.

  2. Differential Closure: If $\alpha \in \mathcal{I}$, then $d\alpha \in \mathcal{I}$.

An exterior differential system is a pair $(M, \mathcal{I})$. In the context of PDE theory, the manifold $M$ is typically the jet bundle $J^k(E)$. The ideal $\mathcal{I}$ is generated by:

  1. The contact forms $\theta^\alpha = du^\alpha - p^\alpha_i dx^i$, which enforce the geometry of the jet bundle.

  2. The $0$-forms (functions) $F^\beta: J^k(E) \to \mathbb{R}$ representing the PDE constraints.

  3. The exterior derivatives of these forms, required to satisfy differential closure.

The condition of differential closure ($d\mathcal{I} \subset \mathcal{I}$) is crucial. It encapsulates all integrability conditions and hidden constraints obtained by differentiating the original system, analogous to the prolongation procedures discussed in Section 6.2.

Definition 6.3.2 (Integral Manifold). An integral manifold of an exterior differential system $(M, \mathcal{I})$ is a submanifold $N \subset M$ and an immersion $\iota: N \hookrightarrow M$ such that the pullback of every form in the ideal vanishes:

\[\iota^* \alpha = 0 \quad \text{for all } \alpha \in \mathcal{I}.\]

For a PDE system, a classical solution corresponds to an integral manifold that is transverse to the projection $\pi: J^k(E) \to X$ (the independent variables). This definition unifies various classes of differential equations; for instance, Frobenius‚Äô theorem is simply the study of integral manifolds for an ideal generated entirely by 1-forms. The geometric machinery of differential ideals is covered extensively in Olver (1995) and Spivak (1979).

Integral Elements and the Cartan Test

To construct integral manifolds, we proceed pointwise. We essentially construct the solution by assembling ‚Äúvalid tangent planes‚Äù at every point.

Definition 6.3.3 (Integral Element). A linear subspace $E \subset T_p M$ is an integral element of $\mathcal{I}$ if for all $\alpha \in \mathcal{I}$:

\[\alpha(v_1, \dots, v_k) = 0 \quad \text{for all } v_1, \dots, v_k \in E.\]

The set of all $k$-dimensional integral elements at $p$ forms a subset of the Grassmannian $Gr_k(T_p M)$. Constructing a solution involves finding a sequence of integral elements $E_1 \subset E_2 \subset \dots \subset E_n$ (a flag) and integrating them into a manifold.

The obstruction to extending an integral element $E_k$ to a higher dimension $E_{k+1}$ is measured by the polar space.

Definition 6.3.4 (Polar Space). Let $E$ be an integral element at $p$. The polar space $H(E)$ is the set of vectors $v \in T_p M$ such that $\text{span}(E \cup {v})$ remains an integral element.

The dimension of the polar space determines the freedom we have in extending the solution. Cartan introduced a set of integers $s_0, s_1, \dots, s_n$, known as Cartan characters, to quantify this freedom. The character $s_k$ roughly counts the number of new constraints appearing at dimension $k$.

Theorem 6.3.1 (Cartan‚Äôs Test for Involutivity). An exterior differential system is involutive (or regular) at an integral element $E_n$ if the dimension of the polar space satisfies a specific equality relating to the Cartan characters. Specifically, if $c_n$ is the codimension of the integral element manifold in the Grassmannian, involutivity requires:

\[\text{dim } V_n(E) = \sum_{k=1}^n k s_k.\]

In simpler terms, a system is involutive if there are no algebraic obstructions to constructing the solution flag step-by-step. If the test fails, the system is singular or requires prolongation to expose further constraints. This linear algebraic criterion for solvability is a central result in Olver (1995).

The Cartan-K√§hler Theorem

The Cartan-K√§hler theorem is the fundamental existence result for exterior differential systems. It establishes that if a system is algebraically consistent (involutive) and the data is real-analytic, local solutions are guaranteed to exist.

Theorem 6.3.2 (Cartan-K√§hler). Let $(M, \mathcal{I})$ be a real-analytic exterior differential system. Let $P \subset M$ be a connected, $k$-dimensional analytic integral manifold. If the system is involutive along $P$, then there exists a unique, local, analytic $(k+1)$-dimensional integral manifold $N$ containing $P$.

By induction, this allows for the construction of integral manifolds of dimension $n$ (solutions to the PDE).

Proof Concept:

The proof relies on the Cauchy-Kovalevskaya theorem (Covered in Evans, 2010). The Cartan test ensures that we can choose a coordinate system where the exterior differential system can be rewritten as a determined system of Cauchy-Kovalevskaya type evolution equations. The step-by-step extension of integral elements $E_k \to E_{k+1}$ corresponds to solving a sequence of analytic Cauchy problems.

Significance:

  1. Generality: It applies to undetermined, determined, and overdetermined systems alike, provided they are involutive.

  2. Geometric Nature: It constructs the solution strictly from the geometry of the ideal $\mathcal{I}$, independent of the choice of independent/dependent variables.

  3. Analyticity Constraint: Like Cauchy-Kovalevskaya, it requires analytic data. It cannot guarantee existence for $C^\infty$ (smooth) data; systems that satisfy Cartan-K√§hler but fail for smooth data usually exhibit Lewy-type instabilities.

This theorem represents the pinnacle of local existence theory for geometric PDEs. It resolves the question of existence by reducing it to a linear algebra problem (computing ranks of polar matrices to check involutivity) and a check of analyticity. When the system is not involutive, one must prolong the system (differentiate the ideal) until it becomes involutive or contradictory, a process known as the Cartan-Kuranishi prolongation algorithm.

Complete Examples

Example 6.3.1: Canonical Example‚ÄîThe Classical Gradient Ideal

Problem: Find all functions $u: \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$ such that $u_x = f(x,y)$ for a given smooth function $f$.

Step-by-Step Solution:

  1. EDS Construction: Consider $M = \mathbb{R}^3$ with coordinates $(x,y,u)$. The exterior differential system is generated by:

    \[\mathcal{I} = \{ \theta = du - f(x,y)dx \}\]

  2. Verify Differential Closure: Compute $d\theta$:

    \[d\theta = d(du - f dx) = -df \wedge dx = -\left(f_x dx + f_y dy\right) \wedge dx = -f_y dy \wedge dx\]

    Since $dy \wedge dx = -dx \wedge dy$, we have:

    \[d\theta = f_y dx \wedge dy\]

    Is $d\theta \in \mathcal{I}$? We need to check if $f_y dx \wedge dy$ can be expressed using $\theta$ and wedge products:

    \[d\theta = f_y dx \wedge dy = f_y dx \wedge (du - \theta) = f_y dx \wedge du - f_y dx \wedge \theta\]

    Since $dx \wedge \theta \in \mathcal{I}$, we only need $f_y dx \wedge du \in \mathcal{I}$. This requires:

    \[\mathcal{I}^{(2)} = \{ \alpha \wedge \beta \mid \alpha \in \mathcal{I}, \beta \in \Omega^*(M) \}\]

    Thus $dx \wedge \theta \in \mathcal{I}^{(2)}$, so $\mathcal{I}$ is differentially closed.

  3. Integral Manifolds: An integral manifold $N \subset \mathbb{R}^3$ satisfies $\iota^*\theta = 0$, where $\iota: N \hookrightarrow \mathbb{R}^3$. Parametrize $N$ by $(x,y)$:

    \[u = u(x,y) \implies du = u_x dx + u_y dy\]

    Then:

    \[\iota^*\theta = (u_x dx + u_y dy) - f dx = (u_x - f)dx + u_y dy = 0\]

    This gives the Pfaffian system:

    \[\begin{cases} u_x = f(x,y) \\ u_y = 0 \end{cases}\]

  4. Solution: $u(x,y) = \int_0^x f(t,y) dt + g(y)$, but $u_y = 0$ implies $g‚Äô(y) = 0$, so:

    \[u(x,y) = \int_0^x f(t,y) dt + c\]

    Geometric Interpretation: Solutions are graphs of functions whose $x$-derivative matches $f$.

Example 6.3.2: Physical Application‚ÄîIncompressible Flow

Problem: Find all divergence-free vector fields $\mathbf{v} = (P,Q)$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$.

Step-by-Step Solution:

  1. EDS Construction: $M = \mathbb{R}^4$ with coordinates $(x,y,P,Q)$. The EDS is:

    \[\mathcal{I} = \{ \omega = P_y - Q_x \}\]

  2. Differential Closure: \(d\omega = d(P_y - Q_x) = P_{yx} dx \wedge dy - Q_{xy} dy \wedge dx = 0\)

    Since $P_{yx} = Q_{xy}$ (equality of mixed partials), $d\omega = 0 \in \mathcal{I}$.

  3. Integral Elements: At each point $(x_0,y_0,P_0,Q_0)$, find $E \subset T_{(x_0,y_0,P_0,Q_0)}M$ such that:

    \[\omega(v_1,v_2) = 0 \quad \forall v_1,v_2 \in E\]

    The condition $P_y = Q_x$ constrains the $(x,y)$-directions. Integral manifolds are surfaces where:

    \[P(x,y), Q(x,y) \text{ satisfy } \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}\]

  4. Complete Solution: Set $P(x,y) = \int_0^y f(x,t) dt + g(x)$.

    Then $Q_x = P_y = f(x,y)$.

    Integrate: $Q(x,y) = \int_0^x f(s,y) ds + h(y)$.

    General solution: $\mathbf{v} = \nabla^\perp \psi$ for some stream function $\psi$.

Example 6.3.3: Advanced Demonstration‚ÄîSurface of Revolution PDE

Problem: Solve $z_{xy} = F(x,y,z,z_x,z_y)$ for a surface $z = z(x,y)$.

Step-by-Step Solution:

  1. EDS Construction: $M = J^1(\mathbb{R}^2,\mathbb{R})$ with coordinates $(x,y,z,p,q)$. The ideal is:

    \[\mathcal{I} = \left\{ \theta = dz - p dx - q dy, \quad \omega = dp \wedge dx + dq \wedge dy - F dx \wedge dy \right\}\]

  2. Contact Structure: $\theta = 0$ enforces $(p,q) = (z_x,z_y)$.

  3. Integral Elements: Find 2-planes $E \subset T_{(x,y,z,p,q)}M$ such that:
    • $E$ annihilates $\theta$: $\theta _E = 0$
    • $E$ annihilates $\omega$: $\omega _E = 0$

    Basis for $T M$: $\partial_x, \partial_y, \partial_z, \partial_p, \partial_q$

    Condition 1: $\theta(\partial_x) = -p$, $\theta(\partial_y) = -q$, $\theta(\partial_z) = 1$

    An integral element must lie in $\ker \theta$, spanned by:

    \[v_1 = \partial_x + p \partial_z, \quad v_2 = \partial_y + q \partial_z, \quad v_3 = \partial_p, \quad v_4 = \partial_q\]

    Condition 2: Evaluate $\omega$ on this distribution:

    \[\omega(v_1,v_2) = \omega(\partial_x + p\partial_z, \partial_y + q\partial_z) = -F\]

    Thus $\omega(v_1,v_2) = -F \neq 0$ unless $F = 0$.

  4. Corrected Integral Elements: We need $E = \text{span}{v_1,v_2}$ where:

    \[v_1 = \partial_x + p \partial_z + a \partial_p + b \partial_q\] \[v_2 = \partial_y + q \partial_z + c \partial_p + d \partial_q\]

  5. Cartan Test Application: The polar space $H(E)$ consists of vectors $w$ such that $\text{span}(E \cup {w})$ remains integral. Compute:

    \[\omega(v_1,v_2) = 0, \quad \omega(v_1,w) = 0, \quad \omega(v_2,w) = 0\]

    This gives a $2 \times 4$ matrix whose rank determines involutivity.

    Cartan characters: $s_0 = 1$ (freedom in $z$ direction), $s_1 = 1$ (freedom in $(p,q)$ plane)

    Involutivity Condition:

    \[\dim V_2(E) = 2s_1 + 1s_2 = 2 + 1 = 3\]

    If the rank condition holds, the system is involutive.

Example 6.3.4: Monge-Amp√®re Equation

Problem: $z_{xx}z_{yy} - z_{xy}^2 = 1$ (special case).

Step-by-Step Solution:

  1. EDS: \(\mathcal{I} = \{ \theta = dz - p dx - q dy, \omega_1 = dp - r dx - s dy,\)

    \[\omega_2 = dq - s dx - t dy, \omega_3 = r t - s^2 - 1 \}\]

  2. Cartan Test Computation (at generic point): Integral element: $E_2 = \text{span}{\partial_x + p\partial_z + r\partial_p + s\partial_q, \partial_y + q\partial_z + s\partial_p + t\partial_q}$

    Polar vectors: Solve $\omega_i(E_2,v) = 0$ for $i=1,2,3$

    Freedom count: $s_0 = 1$ (initial $z$), $s_1 = 2$ (initial $(p,q)$), $s_2 = 0$ (second derivatives determined)

  3. Verification: $\dim V_2 = 1 \cdot 2 + 2 \cdot 0 = 2$, involutive.

Example 6.3.5: Complete Local Existence‚ÄîGoursat Problem

Problem: Solve the nonlinear wave equation:

\[u_{xy} = f(x,y,u,u_x,u_y)\]

Initial Data: $u(x,0) = \phi(x)$, $u(0,y) = \psi(y)$

Step-by-Step Solution:

  1. EDS Construction: $M = J^1(\mathbb{R}^2,\mathbb{R})$, coordinates $(x,y,u,p,q)$

    \[\mathcal{I} = \{ \theta = du - p dx - q dy, \omega = dp \wedge dy - f dx \wedge dy \}\]

  2. Verify Involutivity: Integral elements at $(x_0,y_0,u_0,p_0,q_0)$:

    $E = \text{span}{v_1 = \partial_x + p_0 \partial_u + a \partial_p + b \partial_q,$

    $v_2 = \partial_y + q_0 \partial_u + c \partial_p + d \partial_q}$

    Conditions: $\theta(v_1) = \theta(v_2) = 0$ (automatic), $\omega(v_1,v_2) = 0$

    Compute:

    \[\omega(v_1,v_2) = \omega(\partial_x,\partial_y) + \text{terms involving } a,b,c,d = -f + (d - b)\]

    Set $d = b$. Polar space dimension: 3 parameters $(a,b,c)$.

    Cartan characters: $s_0 = 1$, $s_1 = 1$

    Involutivity: $\dim V_2 = 1 \cdot 1 = 1$, satisfied.

  3. Cartan-K√§hler Application: Initial manifold $P_1$: Along $y=0$, $u = \phi(x)$, $p = \phi‚Äô(x)$, $q$ free.

    Extension to $P_2$: Solve for $q(x,0)$ using $\omega _P = 0$:
    \[dp \wedge dy = f dx \wedge dy \implies \frac{\partial p}{\partial y}\bigg|_{y=0} = f(x,0,\phi(x),\phi'(x),q)\]

    This is an ODE for $q(x,0)$:

    \[\frac{dq}{dx} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial p} \phi''(x) + \frac{\partial f}{\partial q}\]
  4. Analytic Solution (assuming $f$ analytic): By Cauchy-Kovalevskaya, local solution exists. Cartan-K√§hler guarantees:
    • Unique analytic extension to $(x,y)$-plane
    • Dimension increases: $P_1 \to P_2$ (full solution)

  5. Explicit Verification for $f = u u_x u_y$: Initial data: $\phi(x) = x^2$, $\psi(y) = y^2$

    Along $y=0$: $p = 2x$, solve $q_x = 2x \cdot q$

    $q(x,0) = c e^{x^2}$

    Full solution constructed iteratively.

Example 6.3.6: Overdetermined System‚ÄîFailure Case

Problem: $u_{xx} = 0$, $u_{xy} = 0$ (incompatible unless $u_{yy} = 0$).

Step-by-Step Solution:

  1. EDS: \(\mathcal{I} = \{ \theta = dz - p dx - q dy, \omega_1 = dp, \omega_2 = dp \wedge dy \}\)

  2. Cartan Test: $E_2 = \text{span}{\partial_x + p\partial_z, \partial_y + q\partial_z}$

    $\omega_1(E_2, \cdot)$: $\omega_1(\partial_x + p\partial_z, v) = 0$ for all $v$

    Polar space: Requires $dp = 0$ on entire $T M$

  3. Obstruction: $\dim V_2 = 0 < 1$ (not involutive).

  4. Prolongation: Add $d\omega_2 = 0 \implies d^2p = 0$, revealing full Laplace equation.

Example 6.3.7: Sophus Lie‚Äôs Contact System

Problem: Find all surfaces with Gaussian curvature $K = 1$.

Step-by-Step Solution:

  1. EDS (in $J^2$): \(\mathcal{I} = \{ \theta = dz - p dx - q dy, \omega_1 = dp - r dx - s dy,\)

    \[\omega_2 = dq - s dx - t dy, \omega_3 = (r t - s^2) - 1 \}\]

  2. Complete Cartan Analysis: Characters: $s_0 = 1$, $s_1 = 1$, $s_2 = 0$

    Involutive: Yes (standard result)

  3. Solutions: Spheres $x^2 + y^2 + (z-a)^2 = R^2$

    Geometric Insight: Cartan-K√§hler constructs spheres from initial curves with correct first/second fundamental forms.
