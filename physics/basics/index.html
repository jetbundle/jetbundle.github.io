variational calculus and symmetries

the equations of motion and conservation laws for any closed system arise with absolute necessity once the geometric symmetries of space and time are enforced. what must the dynamical law be, given that space is homogeneous and isotropic while time is homogeneous? the structure that satisfies these requirements is the principle of least action, the euler-lagrange equations, and the galilean invariance of the lagrangian (mechanics, landau & lifshitz vol. 1, secs. 1–3).

the principle of least action and the calculus of variations

any mechanical system with \(s\) degrees of freedom is completely specified at each instant by generalized coordinates \(q_i\) and generalized velocities \(\dot{q}_i\). between fixed times \(t_1\) and \(t_2\), the system must follow one particular trajectory. the action is defined as

\[ S = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} L(q_i, \dot{q}_i, t)\, dt, \]

where \(L\) is the lagrangian. why must it be the trajectory that makes the action integral stationary? to consider any other conceivable path would violate the symmetries already built into the description of space and time; and the stationary path is the unique one consistent with those symmetries. lets demonstrate this.

to extract the equations of motion, introduce an arbitrary infinitesimal variation \(\delta q_i(t)\) about the true path, subject to the strict boundary conditions \(\delta q_i(t_1)=\delta q_i(t_2)=0\). the first variation of the action must vanish:

\[ \delta S = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \sum_{i=1}^{s} \left( \frac{\partial L}{\partial q_i}\delta q_i + \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_i}\delta \dot{q}_i \right)dt = 0. \]

because \(\delta \dot{q}_i = \frac{d}{dt}\delta q_i\), the velocity term requires integration by parts:

\[ \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_i} \frac{d}{dt}\left(\delta q_i\right)\, dt = \left[\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_i}\delta q_i\right]_{t_1}^{t_2} - \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_i}\right)\delta q_i\, dt. \]

the boundary term vanishes identically by the fixed-endpoint condition. substituting back and collecting coefficients of the arbitrary \(\delta q_i\) yields

\[ \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \sum_{i=1}^{s} \left( \frac{\partial L}{\partial q_i} - \frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_i} \right)\delta q_i\, dt = 0. \]

since the variations are completely arbitrary inside the interval, the integrand itself must be identically zero for each \(i\). this births the euler-lagrange equations out of necessity for the mathematical statement to remain consistent.

\[ \frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_i}\right)-\frac{\partial L}{\partial q_i}=0. \]

these equations are not simply assumed, but they are the consequence of our demands for the the action to be stationary under arbitrary variations. The deep idea develops into this: respect the boundary conditions required by the symmetries of the problem.

geometric symmetries and the free particle lagrangian

the fact should be reasoned through that the lagrangian of a free particle cannot be chosen arbitrarily. every functional dependence is fixed by the symmetries of space and time. space is homogeneous, so \(L\) cannot depend on the position vector \(\mathbf{r}\). space is isotropic, so \(L\) cannot depend on the direction of the velocity \(\mathbf{v}\); it can depend only on the scalar \(v^2\). time is homogeneous, so \(L\) cannot depend explicitly on \(t\). thus \(L=L(v^2)\).

galilean relativity now supplies the final constraint. the equations of motion must be unchanged under an infinitesimal boost to a new inertial frame moving at velocity \(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}\). in the new frame the velocity becomes \(\mathbf{v}'=\mathbf{v}+\boldsymbol{\epsilon}\). expanding to first order gives

\[ L(v'^2)=L\!\left(v^2+2\mathbf{v}\cdot\boldsymbol{\epsilon}+\boldsymbol{\epsilon}^2\right) \approx L(v^2)+2\frac{\partial L}{\partial v^2}\left(\mathbf{v}\cdot\boldsymbol{\epsilon}\right). \]

the physics must be identical despite such coordinate transformations. more precisely, the difference between the two lagrangians must not change the stationary path. this leads one to find that two lagrangians yield identical euler-lagrange equations if and only if they differ by the total time derivative of some scalar function \(f(q,t)\). it's important to see why. consider \(L' = L + \frac{df}{dt}\). the corresponding action changes by

\[ S' = S + f(q(t_2),t_2)-f(q(t_1),t_1). \]

the variation \(\delta S'\) therefore receives only boundary terms that vanish because the endpoints are fixed. hence \(\delta S'=\delta S=0\).

the extra term \(2\frac{\partial L}{\partial v^2}\left(\mathbf{v}\cdot\boldsymbol{\epsilon}\right)\) must therefore itself be a total time derivative. substituting \(\mathbf{v}=d\mathbf{r}/dt\) produces

\[ 2\frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt}\cdot\boldsymbol{\epsilon}\,\frac{\partial L}{\partial v^2} = \frac{d}{dt}\left(2\mathbf{r}\cdot\boldsymbol{\epsilon}\,\frac{\partial L}{\partial v^2}\right) \]

if and only if \(\frac{\partial L}{\partial v^2}\) is independent of \(\mathbf{v}\). the only possibility is that this coefficient is a true constant, conventionally written \(\frac{1}{2}m\). the free-particle lagrangian is therefore revealed to be

\[ L=\frac{1}{2}mv^2. \]

this is the kinetic energy. when interactions are present, the potential \(u(\mathbf{r})\) enters with a minus sign, yielding the universal form \(L=T-U\).

symmetries and conservation laws

the same geometric symmetries that fixed the lagrangian now force the existence of conserved quantities. each symmetry is translated into a precise mathematical constraint on \(L\), and the euler-lagrange equations then convert that constraint into a conservation law.

conservation of energy (homogeneity of time)

homogeneity of time requires \(\frac{\partial L}{\partial t}=0\). the total time derivative of \(L\) is

\[ \frac{d L}{dt}=\sum_i \frac{\partial L}{\partial q_i}\dot{q}_i+\sum_i \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_i}\ddot{q}_i. \]

replace \(\frac{\partial L}{\partial q_i}\) by \(\frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_i}\right)\) using the euler-lagrange equations:

\[ \frac{d L}{dt}=\sum_i\left(\dot{q}_i\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_i}+\ddot{q}_i\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_i}\right) =\sum_i \frac{d}{dt}\left(\dot{q}_i\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_i}\right). \]

rearrangement immediately isolates a quantity whose time derivative vanishes:

\[ \frac{d}{dt}\left(\sum_i \dot{q}_i\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_i}-L\right)=0. \]

the expression in parentheses is the total energy \(E\).

conservation of momentum (homogeneity of space)

homogeneity of space requires invariance under an arbitrary infinitesimal translation \(\delta \mathbf{r}\). the variation of the lagrangian must vanish:

\[ \delta L=\sum_a \nabla_a L \cdot \delta \mathbf{r}=0. \]

because \(\delta \mathbf{r}\) is arbitrary, \(\sum_a \nabla_a L = 0\). substituting the euler-lagrange equations converts this into

\[ \sum_a \frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{v}_a}\right) =\frac{d}{dt}\sum_a \mathbf{p}_a = 0, \]

so the total linear momentum \(\mathbf{P}=\sum_a \mathbf{p}_a\) is conserved.

conservation of angular momentum (isotropy of space)

isotropy requires invariance under an infinitesimal rotation \(\delta \boldsymbol{\phi}\). the coordinate and velocity changes are

\[ \delta \mathbf{r}_a = \delta \boldsymbol{\phi}\times \mathbf{r}_a, \qquad \delta \mathbf{v}_a = \delta \boldsymbol{\phi}\times \mathbf{v}_a. \]

the condition \(\delta L=0\) becomes

\[ \sum_a\left( \frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{r}_a}\cdot(\delta \boldsymbol{\phi}\times \mathbf{r}_a) + \frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{v}_a}\cdot(\delta \boldsymbol{\phi}\times \mathbf{v}_a) \right)=0. \]

insert the euler-lagrange identifications \(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{r}_a}=\dot{\mathbf{p}}_a\) and \(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{v}_a}=\mathbf{p}_a\), then apply the cyclic property of the scalar triple product:

\[ \delta \boldsymbol{\phi}\cdot \sum_a\left(\mathbf{r}_a\times \dot{\mathbf{p}}_a + \mathbf{v}_a\times \mathbf{p}_a\right) = \delta \boldsymbol{\phi}\cdot \frac{d}{dt}\sum_a\left(\mathbf{r}_a\times \mathbf{p}_a\right) =0. \]

since \(\delta \boldsymbol{\phi}\) is arbitrary, the total angular momentum \(\mathbf{M}=\sum_a \mathbf{r}_a\times \mathbf{p}_a\) is conserved.

these three conservation laws are not separate postulates; they are the direct, unavoidable consequences of the three continuous geometric symmetries once the variational structure is in place. the table below summarizes the exact mapping:

continuous geometric symmetry mathematical constraint on lagrangian conserved physical quantity
homogeneity of time \(\frac{\partial L}{\partial t}=0\) total energy \(E\)
homogeneity of space \(\sum_a \nabla_a L = 0\) linear momentum \(\mathbf{P}\)
isotropy of space invariance under \(\delta \boldsymbol{\phi}\) rotation angular momentum \(\mathbf{M}\)

relativistic extension

to prepare the ground for field theory, the galilean framework must be superseded by lorentz invariance. the action for a free relativistic particle must be a scalar that is the same in every inertial frame. the only geometric object available with this property is the spacetime interval \(ds\) itself (the classical theory of fields, landau & lifshitz vol. 2, sec. 8). the action is therefore

\[ S=-mc\int_a^b ds, \]

where \(ds=c\sqrt{1-v^2/c^2}\, dt\). expanding yields the relativistic lagrangian

\[ L=-mc^2\sqrt{1-\frac{v^2}{c^2}}. \]

this form is forced by the requirement that the action be lorentz-invariant; no other dependence on velocity satisfies the symmetry. it stands as the necessary starting point for all subsequent developments involving fields and their interaction with matter.

reduction and effective potentials

once the variational structure is in place, a conservative interaction that depends only on the scalar separation of two particles forces an exact reduction to a one-body radial problem. the same geometric symmetries already present turn the plane confinement into a centrifugal barrier, and the orbit and scattering data become functions of an effective potential alone.

the two-body central force problem and linear reduction

consider two particles interacting through a potential \(u\) that depends only on the magnitude of their separation, \(u(|\mathbf{r}_1-\mathbf{r}_2|)\). in an inertial frame the lagrangian is

\[ L=\frac{1}{2}m_1\dot{\mathbf{r}}_1^2+\frac{1}{2}m_2\dot{\mathbf{r}}_2^2-u(|\mathbf{r}_1-\mathbf{r}_2|). \]

because the potential is translationally invariant, the total momentum is conserved and the center-of-mass motion decouples exactly. to see why the separation works, introduce the linear change of variables

\[ \mathbf{r}=\mathbf{r}_1-\mathbf{r}_2,\qquad \mathbf{R}=\frac{m_1\mathbf{r}_1+m_2\mathbf{r}_2}{m_1+m_2}. \]

inverting gives

\[ \mathbf{r}_1=\mathbf{R}+\frac{m_2}{m_1+m_2}\mathbf{r},\qquad \mathbf{r}_2=\mathbf{R}-\frac{m_1}{m_1+m_2}\mathbf{r}. \]

differentiate with respect to time and substitute into the kinetic energy:

\[ T=\frac{1}{2}m_1\left(\dot{\mathbf{R}}+\frac{m_2}{m_1+m_2}\dot{\mathbf{r}}\right)^2 +\frac{1}{2}m_2\left(\dot{\mathbf{R}}-\frac{m_1}{m_1+m_2}\dot{\mathbf{r}}\right)^2. \]

expanding the squares, every cross term \(\dot{\mathbf{R}}\cdot\dot{\mathbf{r}}\) cancels identically. the result is

\[ T=\frac{1}{2}M\dot{\mathbf{R}}^2+\frac{1}{2}\mu\dot{\mathbf{r}}^2, \]

where \(M=m_1+m_2\) is the total mass and \(\mu=\frac{m_1m_2}{m_1+m_2}\) is the reduced mass. the lagrangian therefore becomes

\[ L=\frac{1}{2}M\dot{\mathbf{R}}^2+\frac{1}{2}\mu\dot{\mathbf{r}}^2-u(r). \]

the center-of-mass coordinate \(\mathbf{R}\) appears only in the free kinetic term and nowhere in the potential; it is cyclic. its conjugate momentum is conserved, and its motion is uniform and rectilinear. dropping the irrelevant center-of-mass kinetic energy leaves the dynamics of a single fictitious particle of mass \(\mu\) moving in the central field \(u(r)\):

\[ L=\frac{1}{2}\mu\dot{\mathbf{r}}^2-u(r). \]

this reduction is forced: the functional form of the potential guarantees that the two-body problem is mathematically equivalent to a one-body problem in the relative vector.

conservation laws and the effective potential

the potential \(u(r)\) is spherically symmetric, so the isotropy of space remains intact. the total angular momentum \(\mathbf{M}\) of the relative motion is therefore conserved. its direction is fixed, confining the motion to a plane perpendicular to \(\mathbf{M}\), and its magnitude fixes the constant areal velocity.

in polar coordinates \((r,\phi)\) within that plane, the lagrangian reads

\[ L=\frac{1}{2}\mu\left(\dot{r}^2+r^2\dot{\phi}^2\right)-u(r). \]

the angle \(\phi\) is cyclic: it does not appear explicitly. its conjugate momentum

\[ M_z=\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{\phi}}=\mu r^2\dot{\phi} \]

is therefore constant, equal to the conserved angular momentum. solving for \(\dot{\phi}\) gives

\[ \dot{\phi}=\frac{M_z}{\mu r^2}. \]

the total energy \(E\) (from time homogeneity) is

\[ E=\frac{1}{2}\mu\dot{r}^2+\frac{1}{2}\mu r^2\dot{\phi}^2+u(r). \]

substituting \(\dot{\phi}=\frac{M_z}{\mu r^2}\) yields

\[ E=\frac{1}{2}\mu\dot{r}^2+\frac{M_z^2}{2\mu r^2}+u(r). \]

the radial motion becomes one-dimensional under the effective potential

\[ U_{\text{eff}}(r)=u(r)+\frac{M_z^2}{2\mu r^2}. \]

the term \(\frac{M_z^2}{2\mu r^2}\) is the centrifugal barrier. it is not an added force; it is the purely geometric consequence of expressing the conserved angular momentum in radial coordinates. every central-force problem therefore reduces to an effective one-dimensional problem whose qualitative features—bound orbits, turning points, and scattering—are determined entirely by the shape of \(U_{\text{eff}}(r)\).

integration by quadratures and the kepler problem

with energy conserved, solve for the radial velocity:

\[ \dot{r}=\frac{dr}{dt}=\pm\sqrt{\frac{2}{\mu}\left(E-U_{\text{eff}}(r)\right)}. \]

time is obtained by direct quadrature:

\[ t=\int \frac{dr}{\sqrt{\frac{2}{\mu}\left(E-u(r)-\frac{M_z^2}{2\mu r^2}\right)}}+\text{constant}. \]

the orbit equation follows by substituting \(dt\) into \(d\phi=\frac{M_z}{\mu r^2}\,dt\):

\[ \phi=\int \frac{\left(M_z/r^2\right)dr}{\sqrt{2\mu\left(E-u(r)\right)-M_z^2/r^2}}+\text{constant}. \]

for the kepler (or coulomb) problem, \(u(r)=-\frac{\alpha}{r}\). substituting this and changing variable to \(u=1/r\) converts the orbit integral into a standard trigonometric form whose solution gives the conic-section equation

\[ \frac{p}{r}=1+e\cos\phi, \]

where the semi-latus rectum is \(p=\frac{M_z^2}{\mu\alpha}\) and the eccentricity is

\[ e=\sqrt{1+\frac{2EM_z^2}{\mu\alpha^2}}. \]

the value of \(E\) alone classifies the orbit: ellipses when \(E<0\) (and \(e<1\)), parabolas when \(E=0\) (and \(e=1\)), and hyperbolas when \(E>0\) (and \(e>1\)). no other central potential yields closed conic orbits: the \(1/r\) dependence is the unique form that produces exactly this trigonometric relation.

scattering theory and the rutherford cross-section

for positive energy \(E>0\), the trajectories are unbounded hyperbolas. a particle approaching from infinity with impact parameter \(\rho\) (the perpendicular distance from the center to the incoming asymptote) has angular momentum \(M_z=\rho\sqrt{2\mu E}\). the scattering angle \(\chi\) is the angle between incoming and outgoing asymptotes.

because the trajectory is symmetric about the line to the distance of closest approach \(r_{\rm min}\), the scattering angle satisfies

\[ \chi=\left|\pi-2\phi_0\right|, \]

where \(\phi_0\) is the angle swept from \(r_{\rm min}\) to infinity:

\[ \phi_0=\int_{r_{\rm min}}^{\infty} \frac{(\rho/r^2)\,dr}{\sqrt{1-u(r)/E-\rho^2/r^2}}. \]

the differential cross-section \(d\sigma\) is the geometric probability that an incident particle is deflected into the solid angle element \(d\Omega\). particles with impact parameters between \(\rho\) and \(\rho+d\rho\) occupy an area \(2\pi\rho\,d\rho\) and scatter into \(d\Omega=2\pi\sin\chi\,d\chi\). therefore

\[ d\sigma=\frac{\rho}{\sin\chi\,\left|\frac{d\chi}{d\rho}\right|}\,d\Omega. \]

for the repulsive coulomb potential \(u(r)=\alpha/r\) (with \(\alpha=q_1q_2\)), the integral for \(\phi_0\) evaluates to

\[ \rho=\frac{\alpha}{2E}\cot\left(\frac{\chi}{2}\right). \]

differentiate with respect to \(\chi\):

\[ \left|\frac{d\rho}{d\chi}\right|=\frac{\alpha}{4E}\csc^2\left(\frac{\chi}{2}\right). \]

insert this into the cross-section formula and use \(\sin\chi=2\sin(\chi/2)\cos(\chi/2)\). after algebraic simplification, one obtains the exact rutherford scattering cross-section

\[ d\sigma=\left(\frac{\alpha}{4E}\right)^2\frac{1}{\sin^4(\chi/2)}\,d\Omega. \]

this depends only on the incident energy and the charges; it is independent of the masses and connects the microscopic central potential directly to the macroscopic observable scattering angle. every conservative central-force problem is thereby furnished with a complete analytic toolkit: bound orbits classified by energy, unbound trajectories mapped to scattering angles, and measurable cross-sections derived from geometry and conservation alone.

linearization and normal modes

small motion near stable equilibrium is not a pile of coupled springs chosen by hand: it is the unique outcome of expanding both energies to quadratic order while the mass matrix stays positive definite. the same structure repeats for rigid bodies—rotation becomes a quadratic form in \(\boldsymbol{\Omega}\), with a symmetric tensor that always admits principal axes.

multivariable taylor expansion of energy

consider a system with \(s\) degrees of freedom displaced slightly from stable equilibrium at generalized coordinates \(q_{i0}\). define \(x_i=q_i-q_{i0}\). expand the potential \(U(q)\) about this point. stability means a local minimum, so the first derivatives vanish:

\[ \left(\frac{\partial U}{\partial q_i}\right)_0=0. \]

truncate the taylor series at second order and drop the constant \(U(q_0)\). then

\[ U=\frac{1}{2}\sum_{i,k=1}^s k_{ik}x_i x_k, \]

where the stiffness matrix \(K=(k_{ik})\) with \(k_{ik}=\left(\partial^2 U/\partial q_i\partial q_k\right)_0\) is symmetric. stability requires \(U\) to increase for any nonzero displacement, so \(K\) is positive definite.

the kinetic energy \(T=\frac{1}{2}\sum a_{ik}(q)\dot{q}_i\dot{q}_k\) is expanded consistently: at second order the coefficients are evaluated at equilibrium, giving constants \(m_{ik}\) and

\[ T=\frac{1}{2}\sum_{i,k=1}^s m_{ik}\dot{x}_i\dot{x}_k. \]

the mass matrix \(M=(m_{ik})\) is symmetric and strictly positive definite because \(T>0\) for any nonzero velocity. both energies are quadratic forms; the euler-lagrange equations are linear in the small coordinates \(x_i\).

simultaneous reduction of quadratic forms

the coupled linear equations cannot stay opaque if the true independent modes are to appear. a coordinate change must diagonalize both \(T\) and \(U\) at once. because \(M\) is positive definite, there is a nonsingular map \(x=Cy\) with \(C^T M C=I\), so

\[ T=\frac{1}{2}\sum_i \dot{y}_i^2. \]

under the same change, the potential becomes

\[ U=\frac{1}{2}y^T(C^T K C)y, \]

with \(K'=C^T K C\) still symmetric. any real symmetric matrix is diagonalized by an orthogonal transformation \(y=O\Theta\), \(O^T O=I\), yielding

\[ T=\frac{1}{2}\dot{\Theta}^T\dot{\Theta}=\frac{1}{2}\sum_i\dot{\Theta}_i^2,\qquad U=\frac{1}{2}\sum_i\omega_i^2\Theta_i^2, \]

where \(\omega_i^2\) are the eigenvalues of the reduced problem. in normal coordinates \(\Theta_i\) the system splits into \(s\) independent oscillators,

\[ \ddot{\Theta}_i+\omega_i^2\Theta_i=0. \]

the frequencies obey the secular equation from the original matrices,

\[ \det(k_{ik}-\omega^2 m_{ik})=0. \]

this eigenproblem is forced: simultaneous diagonalization is the unique algebraic route that respects positive-definite kinetic energy while diagonalizing the potential. no other linear change of coordinates decouples the motion without distorting the physical meaning of \(T\) and \(U\).

rigid body kinematics and the inertia tensor

for a rigid body with fixed mutual distances, each mass element has velocity \(\mathbf{v}=\boldsymbol{\Omega}\times\mathbf{r}\) for angular velocity \(\boldsymbol{\Omega}\). thus

\[ T=\frac{1}{2}\int \rho(\boldsymbol{\Omega}\times\mathbf{r})^2\,dV. \]

using \((\mathbf{A}\times\mathbf{B})^2=A^2B^2-(\mathbf{A}\cdot\mathbf{B})^2\),

\[ T=\frac{1}{2}\int \rho\left[\Omega^2 r^2-(\boldsymbol{\Omega}\cdot\mathbf{r})^2\right]dV. \]

in cartesian components this becomes the quadratic form (with \(r^2=\sum_j x_j^2\))

\[ T=\frac{1}{2}\sum_{i,k}\Omega_i\Omega_k\int \rho\left(r^2\delta_{ik}-x_i x_k\right)dV. \]

the integrals define the inertia tensor

\[ I_{ik}=\int \rho\left(r^2\delta_{ik}-x_i x_k\right)dV, \]

so \(T=\frac{1}{2}\sum_{i,k}I_{ik}\Omega_i\Omega_k\). \(I_{ik}\) is real and symmetric. an orthogonal rotation of body-fixed axes diagonalizes it; in principal axes,

\[ T=\frac{1}{2}\left(I_1\Omega_1^2+I_2\Omega_2^2+I_3\Omega_3^2\right). \]

the principal moments \(I_1,I_2,I_3\) are all one needs for rotational kinetic energy.

poinsot's construction and fictitious forces

for a free asymmetric rigid body (\(I_1\neq I_2\neq I_3\)), conservation of \(E\) and \(\mathbf{M}\) gives two quadratic constraints in principal components:

\[ 2E=I_1\Omega_1^2+I_2\Omega_2^2+I_3\Omega_3^2,\qquad M^2=I_1^2\Omega_1^2+I_2^2\Omega_2^2+I_3^2\Omega_3^2. \]

in \(\boldsymbol{\Omega}\)-space the first surface is the energy ellipsoid; the second is another ellipsoid. \(\boldsymbol{\Omega}\) lies on their intersection; its tip traces polhodes on those surfaces. the geometry of those curves fixes stability: rotation about the intermediate principal axis is unstable; the smallest and largest axes are stable.

in a rotating frame, substitute \(\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{v}'+\boldsymbol{\Omega}\times\mathbf{r}'\) into the free-particle lagrangian:

\[ L=\frac{1}{2}m(\mathbf{v}'+\boldsymbol{\Omega}\times\mathbf{r}')^2-U. \]

expanding,

\[ L=\frac{1}{2}mv'^2+m\mathbf{v}'\cdot(\boldsymbol{\Omega}\times\mathbf{r}') +\frac{1}{2}m(\boldsymbol{\Omega}\times\mathbf{r}')^2-U. \]

the euler-lagrange equations produce the coriolis term \(2m(\mathbf{v}'\times\boldsymbol{\Omega})\) and the centrifugal term \(m\boldsymbol{\Omega}\times(\mathbf{r}'\times\boldsymbol{\Omega})\). these are not new fundamental forces; they keep the equations consistent when the frame rotates. that is the same bridge toward non-inertial frames and later gauge ideas that completes the linearized picture for mechanics built from quadratic forms.

phase space and canonical structure

mechanics does not end in \((q,\dot{q})\): momenta are the right partners for coordinates, and the legendre transform is the unique swap that turns second-order euler-lagrange equations into a symmetric first-order flow. from there, brackets pick out constants, the hamilton–jacobi equation rebuilds families of orbits from a single scalar \(S\), and closed loops in phase space freeze area into adiabatic invariants.

total versus partial derivatives and lagrangian gauge

changing variables demands a clean split between explicit time dependence and motion through the coordinate chart. the partial derivative \(\partial f/\partial t\) holds all \(q\) fixed and records only explicit \(t\) dependence (a drive that shakes the system). along a trajectory,

\[ \frac{df}{dt}=\frac{\partial f}{\partial t}+\sum_i\frac{\partial f}{\partial q_i}\dot{q}_i. \]

if \(\partial L/\partial t\neq 0\), energy is generally not conserved. the earlier gauge fact still holds: two lagrangians differing by a total time derivative \(\frac{d}{dt}f(q,t)\) share the same euler-lagrange equations, because the action shifts only by fixed endpoint terms that vanish under the variational boundary conditions. that freedom keeps the physics insensitive to harmless rewritings of \(L\).

the legendre transform and hamilton's equations

define momenta from \(L\):

\[ p_i=\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_i}. \]

the hamiltonian is the legendre transform

\[ H(q,p,t)=\sum_i p_i\dot{q}_i-L(q,\dot{q},t). \]

to derive the equations, compare differentials. first,

\[ dH=\sum_i\dot{q}_i\,dp_i+\sum_i p_i\,d\dot{q}_i-dL. \]

for \(L\),

\[ dL=\sum_i\frac{\partial L}{\partial q_i}\,dq_i+\sum_i\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_i}\,d\dot{q}_i +\frac{\partial L}{\partial t}\,dt. \]

use \(\partial L/\partial q_i=\dot{p}_i\) from the euler-lagrange equations and \(\partial L/\partial\dot{q}_i=p_i\):

\[ dL=\sum_i\dot{p}_i\,dq_i+\sum_i p_i\,d\dot{q}_i+\frac{\partial L}{\partial t}\,dt. \]

insert into \(dH\): the terms \(p_i\,d\dot{q}_i\) cancel, leaving

\[ dH=\sum_i\dot{q}_i\,dp_i-\sum_i\dot{p}_i\,dq_i-\frac{\partial L}{\partial t}\,dt. \]

but as a function \(H(q,p,t)\), necessarily

\[ dH=\sum_i\frac{\partial H}{\partial p_i}\,dp_i+\sum_i\frac{\partial H}{\partial q_i}\,dq_i+\frac{\partial H}{\partial t}\,dt. \]

matching coefficients gives hamilton's canonical equations:

\[ \dot{q}_i=\frac{\partial H}{\partial p_i},\qquad \dot{p}_i=-\frac{\partial H}{\partial q_i},\qquad \frac{\partial H}{\partial t}=-\frac{\partial L}{\partial t}. \]

the order-one system in \((q,p)\) is symmetric between coordinates and momenta; in this picture phase-space volume is preserved along the flow (liouville's theorem). the structure is forced: the legendre map is the standard algebraic route from lagrangian second-order dynamics to canonical first-order dynamics without discarding what symmetry and variation already fixed.

poisson brackets and integrals of motion

for any observable \(f(q,p,t)\), the chain rule along trajectories gives

\[ \frac{df}{dt}=\frac{\partial f}{\partial t}+\sum_i\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial q_i}\dot{q}_i+\frac{\partial f}{\partial p_i}\dot{p}_i\right). \]

substitute hamilton's equations for \(\dot{q}_i\) and \(\dot{p}_i\):

\[ \frac{df}{dt}=\frac{\partial f}{\partial t}+\sum_i\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial q_i}\frac{\partial H}{\partial p_i}-\frac{\partial f}{\partial p_i}\frac{\partial H}{\partial q_i}\right). \]

the sum is the poisson bracket \(\{f,H\}\):

\[ \{f,H\}=\sum_i\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial q_i}\frac{\partial H}{\partial p_i}-\frac{\partial f}{\partial p_i}\frac{\partial H}{\partial q_i}\right). \]

so

\[ \frac{df}{dt}=\frac{\partial f}{\partial t}+\{f,H\}. \]

if \(\partial f/\partial t=0\) and \(\{f,H\}=0\), then \(df/dt=0\): \(f\) is a constant of the motion. every symmetry-generated integral reappears here as a function whose bracket with \(H\) vanishes; the bracket packages time evolution without solving the flow in closed form.

the hamilton–jacobi equation

treat the action \(S\) not as a single number between fixed endpoints but as a function \(S(q,t)\)—depending on the upper time and the coordinates there. then momenta are the gradients

\[ p_i=\frac{\partial S}{\partial q_i}, \]

and \(\partial S/\partial t=-H\) along the solution. eliminating \(p\) from \(H\) yields the hamilton–jacobi equation

\[ \frac{\partial S}{\partial t}+H\left(q_i,\frac{\partial S}{\partial q_i},t\right)=0. \]

this is a first-order nonlinear pde for \(S\); where separation applies, its solution generates the full family of trajectories—level sets of \(S\) organize the motion, and momenta are read off from \(\nabla_q S\). \(S\) is the generating function for the canonical picture in the sense used throughout analytical mechanics.

action variables and adiabatic invariants

for motion that is conditionally periodic (bounded, returning after finitely many independent cycles), define action variables by contour integrals in phase space, one loop per independent degree of freedom \(k\):

\[ I_k=\frac{1}{2\pi}\oint p_k\,dq_k. \]

these \(I_k\) are constants for the unperturbed motion. for the one-dimensional harmonic oscillator, \(H=p^2/(2m)+\frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 q^2=E\), so \(p=\sqrt{2m(E-\frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 q^2)}\) and the orbit in \((q,p)\) is an ellipse. the contour integral equals the enclosed area divided by \(2\pi\),

\[ I=\frac{E}{\omega}, \]

so \(E=I\omega\). when parameters drift slowly (e.g. \(\omega(t)\) changes on a time scale long compared to a period), the actions remain strict adiabatic invariants: the loop integral is topologically tied to the enclosed phase area, and slow deformation cannot tear that area without breaking the conservation structure already encoded in the hamiltonian picture. that closes the link from periodic geometry to invariants that survive slow change, and points forward to canonical transformations stated in coordinate-free language.

special relativity and the free relativistic particle

non-relativistic mechanics sits in absolute time; field theory does not. the minimal geometric move is lorentz-invariant scalars built on the worldline—then coupling to \(A_i\) as a line integral, a rank-two antisymmetric \(F_{ik}\), gauge redundancy, and finally an action for the field itself so maxwell's equations fall out of the same variational template.

free particle and proper interval

any lorentz scalar tied to a particle path and linear in the worldline element forces the free action to be proportional to the proper interval \(ds\): other choices either break invariance or fail to recover the correct low-energy limit. take

\[ S_m=-mc\int_a^b ds, \]

with

\[ ds=\sqrt{c^2dt^2-dx^2-dy^2-dz^2}=c\sqrt{1-\frac{v^2}{c^2}}\,dt. \]

then

\[ L=-mc^2\sqrt{1-\frac{v^2}{c^2}}. \]

the canonical momentum \(p_i=\partial L/\partial v_i\) gives the relativistic three-momentum

\[ \mathbf{p}=\frac{m\mathbf{v}}{\sqrt{1-v^2/c^2}}. \]

this velocity dependence is fixed: it is the unique lorentz-invariant extension whose small-\(v\) limit matches newtonian kinetic energy. that is the substrate for coupling matter to fields without spoiling spacetime symmetry.

particle-field coupling and the four-potential

to keep covariance, the interaction must be a lorentz scalar built from four-vectors. with four-potential \(A^i=(\phi,\mathbf{A})\) and worldline displacement \(dx^i=(c\,dt,d\mathbf{r})\), minimal coupling is the line integral

\[ S_{mf}=-\frac{e}{c}\int A_i\,dx^i, \]

so the total action is

\[ S=S_m+S_{mf}=\int_a^b\left(-mc\,ds-\frac{e}{c}A_i\,dx^i\right). \]

in laboratory time,

\[ L=-mc^2\sqrt{1-\frac{v^2}{c^2}}+\frac{e}{c}\mathbf{A}\cdot\mathbf{v}-e\phi. \]

differentiation with respect to \(\mathbf{v}\) shows the canonical momentum picks up the vector potential:

\[ \mathbf{P}=\mathbf{p}+\frac{e}{c}\mathbf{A}. \]

this shift is exactly what translation symmetry in the presence of the field demands for the conserved generator; the coupling term is minimal because it is essentially the only lorentz-scalar line integral one can add at linear order in \(A\).

lorentz force and the field tensor

fix \(A_i\) and vary particle coordinates \(x^i\):

\[ \delta S=-\delta\int mc\,ds-\frac{e}{c}\delta\int A_i\,dx^i=0. \]

for the interaction, expand the variation; integrating by parts kills endpoint terms because \(\delta x^i\) vanishes there:

\[ \delta\int A_i\,dx^i=\int\left(\frac{\partial A_i}{\partial x^k}\delta x^k\,dx^i+A_i\,d\delta x^i\right). \]

after relabeling and collecting \(\delta x^i\), arbitrary \(\delta x^i\) forces

\[ mc\frac{du_i}{ds}=\frac{e}{c}F_{ik}u^k, \]

where \(u^i=dx^i/ds\) is the four-velocity (index gymnastics follow the chosen metric convention; the structure is canonical), and the electromagnetic field tensor is the antisymmetric combination

\[ F_{ik}=\frac{\partial A_k}{\partial x^i}-\frac{\partial A_i}{\partial x^k}. \]

in three-vector form this reproduces the lorentz force

\[ \mathbf{f}=e\mathbf{E}+\frac{e}{c}\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{H}, \]

with

\[ \mathbf{E}=-\nabla\phi-\frac{1}{c}\frac{\partial\mathbf{A}}{\partial t},\qquad \mathbf{H}=\nabla\times\mathbf{A}. \]

\(F_{ik}\) is the geometric object encoding the field; antisymmetry and definition through \(A\) are forced if the equations are to follow from a covariant action principle in four dimensions.

gauge invariance and differential forms

\(F_{ik}\) is built from derivatives of \(A\), so observable fields are insensitive to the gauge shift

\[ A_i\to A_i-\partial_i f, \]

with smooth spacetime \(f\). substituting,

\[ F'_{ik}=\partial_i(A_k-\partial_k f)-\partial_k(A_i-\partial_i f) =(\partial_i A_k-\partial_k A_i)-(\partial_i\partial_k f-\partial_k\partial_i f)=F_{ik}, \]

because mixed partials commute. in form language, \(A=A_i\,dx^i\) is a 1-form, \(F=\mathrm{d}A\) a 2-form, and \(A\mapsto A-\mathrm{d}f\). then \(\mathrm{d}(A-\mathrm{d}f)=\mathrm{d}A=F\) since \(\mathrm{d}^2=0\). the redundancy is geometric: many potentials describe one field; continuity of charge and the global consistency of the potentials are tied to the same structure (this is the seed of the topological side of electromagnetism).

field action and maxwell's equations

for the field alone one adds a lorentz-invariant action quadratic in \(F_{ik}\). the natural scalar is \(F_{ik}F^{ik}\), so

\[ S_f=-\frac{1}{16\pi c}\int F_{ik}F^{ik}\,d\Omega,\qquad d\Omega=c\,dt\,dx\,dy\,dz. \]

vary \(S_f+S_{mf}\) with respect to \(A_i\), paths of charges held fixed. after integration by parts (surface terms at infinity taken to vanish),

\[ \delta S_f=\frac{1}{4\pi c}\int \frac{\partial F^{ik}}{\partial x^i}\delta A_k\,d\Omega. \]

generalizing the matter coupling to a continuous four-current \(j^k=(c\rho,\rho\mathbf{v})\), the interaction contributes

\[ \delta S_{mf}=-\frac{1}{c^2}\int j^k\delta A_k\,d\Omega. \]

summing and using arbitrary \(\delta A_k\) gives the covariant inhomogeneous maxwell equations

\[ \partial_i F^{ik}=-\frac{4\pi}{c}j^k. \]

because \(\partial_k\partial_i F^{ik}=0\) for antisymmetric \(F^{ik}\), it follows that \(\partial_k j^k=0\): charge conservation is locked to the same differential identity that makes the field equations consistent.

that single tensor statement packages gauss's law \(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E}=4\pi\rho\) and the ampère–maxwell law \(\nabla\times\mathbf{H}=\frac{4\pi}{c}\mathbf{j}+\frac{1}{c}\frac{\partial\mathbf{E}}{\partial t}\) (in these units). the dynamics of the field is therefore not bolted on: it is the variational completion of the same lorentz-covariant action that already fixed particle motion and the definition of \(F_{ik}\).

waves, radiation, and the physical meaning of integrals

the sourced maxwell equations are not the whole story: \(F=\mathrm{d}A\) forces extra differential identities—no monopoles, faraday coupling circulation to changing flux. from there, stokes and gauss turn infinitesimal statements into macroscopic propagation; retardation enforces \(c\); the wave zone isolates dipole radiation and larmor power; energy balance digs in a self-force that scales with the time derivative of acceleration.

the homogeneous maxwell equations

charges and currents appear in the inhomogeneous pair; the homogeneous pair is kinematics. with

\[ F_{ik}=\partial_i A_k-\partial_k A_i, \]

consider the cyclic sum

\[ \partial_i F_{kl}+\partial_k F_{li}+\partial_l F_{ik}. \]

expand in \(A\): six terms with mixed second derivatives. smoothness gives \(\partial_i\partial_k=\partial_k\partial_i\), so each term cancels and

\[ \partial_i F_{kl}+\partial_k F_{li}+\partial_l F_{ik}=0. \]

with the dual tensor \(\tilde{F}^{ik}=\frac{1}{2}\epsilon^{iklm}F_{lm}\) (\(\epsilon^{iklm}\) totally antisymmetric), this is equivalently

\[ \partial_k\tilde{F}^{ik}=0. \]

in three-vector language: \(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{H}=0\) and faraday \(\nabla\times\mathbf{E}=-\frac{1}{c}\frac{\partial\mathbf{H}}{\partial t}\). these are not new dynamics layered on the action—they are the consistency conditions that follow from antisymmetric \(F_{ik}\) and a local potential.

integral theorems and macroscopic propagation

line integrals \(\oint_C\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{l}\) measure circulation; surface integrals \(\iint_S\mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dS\) measure flux. stokes' theorem gives

\[ \oint_{\partial S}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{l}=\iint_S(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dS. \]

applied to faraday's law,

\[ \oint_{\partial S}\mathbf{E}\cdot d\mathbf{l}=-\frac{1}{c}\frac{d}{dt}\iint_S\mathbf{H}\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dS. \]

the emf around a loop tracks the magnetic flux punched through any spanning surface. the divergence (gauss–ostrogradsky) theorem reads

\[ \iiint_V(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F})\,dV=\oiint_{\partial V}\mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dS. \]

with \(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{H}=0\),

\[ \oiint_{\partial V}\mathbf{H}\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dS=0. \]

magnetic flux through a closed surface vanishes—field lines close. linking induction and divergence to measurable loops and bags of flux is exactly how local maxwell equations become the language of waves in vacuum at speed \(c\).

retarded potentials and liénard–wiechert solutions

the potentials obey the wave operator \(\square=\frac{1}{c^2}\partial_t^2-\nabla^2\):

\[ \square A^i=\frac{4\pi}{c}j^i. \]

signals travel at \(c\), so the field at \((\mathbf{r},t)\) samples sources at retarded time \(t'=t-R/c\), \(R=|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|\). the retarded green's function gives

\[ A^i(\mathbf{r},t)=\frac{1}{c}\int\frac{j^i(\mathbf{r}',t-R/c)}{R}\,dV'. \]

for a point charge \(e\) on \(\mathbf{r}_0(t)\), the integration collapses to the liénard–wiechert forms (evaluated at \(t'\)):

\[ \phi(\mathbf{r},t)=\left.\frac{e}{R-\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{R}/c}\right|_{t'},\qquad \mathbf{A}(\mathbf{r},t)=\left.\frac{e\mathbf{v}}{c\left(R-\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{R}/c\right)}\right|_{t'}, \]

with \(\mathbf{R}=\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}_0\). the denominator is the relativistic beaming factor; retardation enforces strict causality.

multipole expansions and dipole radiation

far from the source (wave zone: \(R\) large compared to size and wavelength), expand the potentials. leading vector-potential piece:

\[ \mathbf{A}=\frac{1}{cR_0}\int\mathbf{j}\,dV\Big|_{t_0}=\frac{\dot{\mathbf{d}}}{cR_0}, \]

where \(\mathbf{d}\) is the electric dipole moment and dots are at retarded time. fields transverse to \(\mathbf{n}\) are

\[ \mathbf{H}=\frac{1}{c^2R_0}(\ddot{\mathbf{d}}\times\mathbf{n}),\qquad \mathbf{E}=\mathbf{H}\times\mathbf{n}. \]

integrating the poynting vector \(\mathbf{S}=\frac{c}{4\pi}\mathbf{E}\times\mathbf{H}\) over a large sphere yields the larmor power

\[ I=\frac{2}{3c^3}\ddot{\mathbf{d}}^2. \]

at this order, radiation comes from accelerating charge; uniform motion or steady currents do not radiate here.

the abraham-lorentz radiation reaction

a radiating charge loses energy to the field; there must be a reaction force \(\mathbf{f}_{\rm rad}\). balance the work done against power radiated (larmor with charge \(e\)):

\[ \int_{t_1}^{t_2}\mathbf{f}_{\rm rad}\cdot\mathbf{v}\,dt =-\int_{t_1}^{t_2}\frac{2e^2}{3c^3}\dot{\mathbf{v}}^2\,dt. \]

rewrite \(\dot{\mathbf{v}}^2=\dot{\mathbf{v}}\cdot\dot{\mathbf{v}}\) and integrate by parts:

\[ \int_{t_1}^{t_2}\dot{\mathbf{v}}\cdot\dot{\mathbf{v}}\,dt =\bigl[\mathbf{v}\cdot\dot{\mathbf{v}}\bigr]_{t_1}^{t_2}-\int_{t_1}^{t_2}\mathbf{v}\cdot\ddot{\mathbf{v}}\,dt. \]

for periodic motion or vanishing boundary contributions,

\[ \int_{t_1}^{t_2}\left(\mathbf{f}_{\rm rad}-\frac{2e^2}{3c^3}\ddot{\mathbf{v}}\right)\cdot\mathbf{v}\,dt=0. \]

so for arbitrary \(\mathbf{v}(t)\) one takes

\[ \mathbf{f}_{\rm rad}=\frac{2e^2}{3c^3}\ddot{\mathbf{v}}. \]

this is proportional to the time derivative of acceleration (jerk of the worldline, up to mass factors in a fuller treatment). it completes the classical radiation picture: homogeneous identities, retarded propagation, dipole larmor, and a self-force that signals where classical electrodynamics needs quantum repair.

General relativity and curved spacetime

Gravity is identified with the geometry of a dynamical manifold. The metric tensor \(g_{ik}\) generalizes the flat Minkowski metric; general covariance demands tensor equations. Covariant derivatives and the Levi-Civita connection are fixed by metric compatibility and vanishing torsion. The Riemann tensor, from the commutator of covariant derivatives, measures curvature via parallel transport. The Einstein–Hilbert action, varied with respect to the metric, yields the Einstein equations linking curvature to the energy–momentum tensor. The Schwarzschild solution is the spherically symmetric vacuum; geodesic motion supplies the effective potential including the relativistic perihelion precession.

Global and topological closure

The local calculus lifts to exterior forms: the potential is a 1-form, field strength \(F = \mathrm{d}A\), and Maxwell’s equations become \(\mathrm{d}F = 0\) and \(\mathrm{d}\star F = \star J\) with the Hodge star \(\star\) in the Lorentzian metric. de Rham cohomology encodes global obstructions to potentials on nontrivial topology. Linearized gravity gives gravitational waves in transverse-traceless gauge, with radiated power tied to the third time derivative of the mass quadrupole. The cosmological principle restricts the metric to FLRW form; Friedmann equations govern the scale factor and imply an initial singularity where curvature and density diverge — the geometric picture of the hot big bang.
