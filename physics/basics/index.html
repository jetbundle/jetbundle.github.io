variational calculus and symmetries

the equations of motion and conservation laws for any closed system arise with absolute necessity once the geometric symmetries of space and time are enforced. what must the dynamical law be, given that space is homogeneous and isotropic while time is homogeneous? the structure that satisfies these requirements is the principle of least action, the euler-lagrange equations, and the galilean invariance of the lagrangian (mechanics, landau & lifshitz vol. 1, secs. 1–3).

the principle of least action and the calculus of variations

any mechanical system with \(s\) degrees of freedom is completely specified at each instant by generalized coordinates \(q_i\) and generalized velocities \(\dot{q}_i\). between fixed times \(t_1\) and \(t_2\), the system must follow one particular trajectory. why must it be the trajectory that makes the action integral stationary? the action is defined as

\[ S = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} L(q_i, \dot{q}_i, t)\, dt, \]

where \(L\) is the lagrangian. any other conceivable path would violate the symmetries already built into the description of space and time; the stationary path is the unique one consistent with those symmetries.

to extract the equations of motion, introduce an arbitrary infinitesimal variation \(\delta q_i(t)\) about the true path, subject to the strict boundary conditions \(\delta q_i(t_1)=\delta q_i(t_2)=0\). the first variation of the action must vanish:

\[ \delta S = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \sum_{i=1}^{s} \left( \frac{\partial L}{\partial q_i}\delta q_i + \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_i}\delta \dot{q}_i \right)dt = 0. \]

because \(\delta \dot{q}_i = \frac{d}{dt}\delta q_i\), the velocity term requires integration by parts:

\[ \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_i} \frac{d}{dt}\left(\delta q_i\right)\, dt = \left[\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_i}\delta q_i\right]_{t_1}^{t_2} - \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_i}\right)\delta q_i\, dt. \]

the boundary term vanishes identically by the fixed-endpoint condition. substituting back and collecting coefficients of the arbitrary \(\delta q_i\) yields

\[ \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \sum_{i=1}^{s} \left( \frac{\partial L}{\partial q_i} - \frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_i} \right)\delta q_i\, dt = 0. \]

since the variations are completely arbitrary inside the interval, the integrand itself must be identically zero for each \(i\). this forces the euler-lagrange equations

\[ \frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_i}\right)-\frac{\partial L}{\partial q_i}=0. \]

these equations are not an assumption; they are the direct mathematical consequence of demanding that the action be stationary under arbitrary variations that respect the boundary conditions required by the symmetries of the problem.

geometric symmetries and the free particle lagrangian

the lagrangian of a free particle cannot be chosen arbitrarily; every functional dependence is fixed by the symmetries of space and time. space is homogeneous, so \(L\) cannot depend on the position vector \(\mathbf{r}\). space is isotropic, so \(L\) cannot depend on the direction of the velocity \(\mathbf{v}\); it can depend only on the scalar \(v^2\). time is homogeneous, so \(L\) cannot depend explicitly on \(t\). thus \(L=L(v^2)\).

galilean relativity now supplies the final constraint. the equations of motion must be unchanged under an infinitesimal boost to a new inertial frame moving at velocity \(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}\). in the new frame the velocity becomes \(\mathbf{v}'=\mathbf{v}+\boldsymbol{\epsilon}\). expanding to first order gives

\[ L(v'^2)=L\!\left(v^2+2\mathbf{v}\cdot\boldsymbol{\epsilon}+\boldsymbol{\epsilon}^2\right) \approx L(v^2)+2\frac{\partial L}{\partial v^2}\left(\mathbf{v}\cdot\boldsymbol{\epsilon}\right). \]

for the physics to be identical, the difference between the two lagrangians must not change the stationary path. two lagrangians yield identical euler-lagrange equations if and only if they differ by the total time derivative of some scalar function \(f(q,t)\). to see why, consider \(L' = L + \frac{df}{dt}\). the corresponding action changes by

\[ S' = S + f(q(t_2),t_2)-f(q(t_1),t_1). \]

the variation \(\delta S'\) therefore receives only boundary terms that vanish because the endpoints are fixed. hence \(\delta S'=\delta S=0\).

the extra term \(2\frac{\partial L}{\partial v^2}\left(\mathbf{v}\cdot\boldsymbol{\epsilon}\right)\) must therefore itself be a total time derivative. substituting \(\mathbf{v}=d\mathbf{r}/dt\) produces

\[ 2\frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt}\cdot\boldsymbol{\epsilon}\,\frac{\partial L}{\partial v^2} = \frac{d}{dt}\left(2\mathbf{r}\cdot\boldsymbol{\epsilon}\,\frac{\partial L}{\partial v^2}\right) \]

if and only if \(\frac{\partial L}{\partial v^2}\) is independent of \(\mathbf{v}\). the only possibility is that this coefficient is a true constant, conventionally written \(\frac{1}{2}m\). the free-particle lagrangian is therefore forced to be

\[ L=\frac{1}{2}mv^2. \]

this is the kinetic energy. when interactions are present, the potential \(u(\mathbf{r})\) enters with a minus sign, yielding the universal form \(L=T-U\).

symmetries and conservation laws

the same geometric symmetries that fixed the lagrangian now force the existence of conserved quantities. each symmetry is translated into a precise mathematical constraint on \(L\), and the euler-lagrange equations then convert that constraint into a conservation law.

conservation of energy (homogeneity of time)

homogeneity of time requires \(\frac{\partial L}{\partial t}=0\). the total time derivative of \(L\) is

\[ \frac{d L}{dt}=\sum_i \frac{\partial L}{\partial q_i}\dot{q}_i+\sum_i \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_i}\ddot{q}_i. \]

replace \(\frac{\partial L}{\partial q_i}\) by \(\frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_i}\right)\) using the euler-lagrange equations:

\[ \frac{d L}{dt}=\sum_i\left(\dot{q}_i\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_i}+\ddot{q}_i\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_i}\right) =\sum_i \frac{d}{dt}\left(\dot{q}_i\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_i}\right). \]

rearrangement immediately isolates a quantity whose time derivative vanishes:

\[ \frac{d}{dt}\left(\sum_i \dot{q}_i\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_i}-L\right)=0. \]

the expression in parentheses is the total energy \(E\).

conservation of momentum (homogeneity of space)

homogeneity of space requires invariance under an arbitrary infinitesimal translation \(\delta \mathbf{r}\). the variation of the lagrangian must vanish:

\[ \delta L=\sum_a \nabla_a L \cdot \delta \mathbf{r}=0. \]

because \(\delta \mathbf{r}\) is arbitrary, \(\sum_a \nabla_a L = 0\). substituting the euler-lagrange equations converts this into

\[ \sum_a \frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{v}_a}\right) =\frac{d}{dt}\sum_a \mathbf{p}_a = 0, \]

so the total linear momentum \(\mathbf{P}=\sum_a \mathbf{p}_a\) is conserved.

conservation of angular momentum (isotropy of space)

isotropy requires invariance under an infinitesimal rotation \(\delta \boldsymbol{\phi}\). the coordinate and velocity changes are

\[ \delta \mathbf{r}_a = \delta \boldsymbol{\phi}\times \mathbf{r}_a, \qquad \delta \mathbf{v}_a = \delta \boldsymbol{\phi}\times \mathbf{v}_a. \]

the condition \(\delta L=0\) becomes

\[ \sum_a\left( \frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{r}_a}\cdot(\delta \boldsymbol{\phi}\times \mathbf{r}_a) + \frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{v}_a}\cdot(\delta \boldsymbol{\phi}\times \mathbf{v}_a) \right)=0. \]

insert the euler-lagrange identifications \(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{r}_a}=\dot{\mathbf{p}}_a\) and \(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{v}_a}=\mathbf{p}_a\), then apply the cyclic property of the scalar triple product:

\[ \delta \boldsymbol{\phi}\cdot \sum_a\left(\mathbf{r}_a\times \dot{\mathbf{p}}_a + \mathbf{v}_a\times \mathbf{p}_a\right) = \delta \boldsymbol{\phi}\cdot \frac{d}{dt}\sum_a\left(\mathbf{r}_a\times \mathbf{p}_a\right) =0. \]

since \(\delta \boldsymbol{\phi}\) is arbitrary, the total angular momentum \(\mathbf{M}=\sum_a \mathbf{r}_a\times \mathbf{p}_a\) is conserved.

these three conservation laws are not separate postulates; they are the direct, unavoidable consequences of the three continuous geometric symmetries once the variational structure is in place. the table below summarizes the exact mapping:

continuous geometric symmetry mathematical constraint on lagrangian conserved physical quantity
homogeneity of time \(\frac{\partial L}{\partial t}=0\) total energy \(E\)
homogeneity of space \(\sum_a \nabla_a L = 0\) linear momentum \(\mathbf{P}\)
isotropy of space invariance under \(\delta \boldsymbol{\phi}\) rotation angular momentum \(\mathbf{M}\)

relativistic extension

to prepare the ground for field theory, the galilean framework must be superseded by lorentz invariance. the action for a free relativistic particle must be a scalar that is the same in every inertial frame. the only geometric object available with this property is the spacetime interval \(ds\) itself (the classical theory of fields, landau & lifshitz vol. 2, sec. 8). the action is therefore

\[ S=-mc\int_a^b ds, \]

where \(ds=c\sqrt{1-v^2/c^2}\, dt\). expanding yields the relativistic lagrangian

\[ L=-mc^2\sqrt{1-\frac{v^2}{c^2}}. \]

this form is forced by the requirement that the action be lorentz-invariant; no other dependence on velocity satisfies the symmetry. it stands as the necessary starting point for all subsequent developments involving fields and their interaction with matter.

Reduction and effective potentials

Conservative central-force systems reduce to an effective one-body problem by separating center-of-mass motion through a linear coordinate transformation. Angular-momentum conservation confines motion to a plane and yields the effective potential with the centrifugal barrier. Integration by quadratures gives the orbit equation; for an inverse-square potential the solutions are conic sections classified by total energy. Scattering trajectories give the Rutherford cross section as a geometric probability from impact-parameter relations. All of this follows from the conservation laws already fixed in the variational framework.

Linearization and normal modes

Near stable equilibrium the potential and kinetic energies are expanded to quadratic order. Positive definiteness of the kinetic-energy matrix forces simultaneous diagonalization of both quadratic forms, producing normal coordinates in which the system decouples into independent harmonic oscillators. The secular equation fixes the normal frequencies. The same quadratic machinery extends to rigid bodies: the inertia tensor from volume integrals and principal axes. Poinsot’s construction governs free rotation stability; fictitious forces in rotating frames bridge toward later gauge ideas.

Phase space and canonical structure

The Legendre transform moves from the Lagrangian on configuration space to the Hamiltonian on phase space. Hamilton’s equations follow from matching differentials after the transform. Poisson brackets encode time evolution and identify integrals of motion. The Hamilton–Jacobi picture treats the action as a generating function whose level surfaces organize trajectories. For periodic systems, action variables from contour integrals are adiabatic invariants under slow parameter change, foreshadowing symplectic geometry.

Special relativity and the free relativistic particle

Field theory needs Lorentz-invariant actions. The free-particle action is proportional to the spacetime interval, giving the relativistic Lagrangian and momentum. Minimal coupling to the electromagnetic four-potential adds the interaction term that shifts the canonical momentum. Varying the total action with respect to particle coordinates yields the Lorentz force and the antisymmetric electromagnetic field tensor \(F_{ik}\). Gauge invariance follows from commutativity of mixed partials; the inhomogeneous Maxwell equations arise from varying the field action with respect to the potential.

Waves, radiation, and the physical meaning of integrals

The homogeneous Maxwell equations are kinematic identities from \(F = \mathrm{d}A\) and \(\mathrm{d}^2 = 0\). Stokes’ theorem and the divergence theorem turn local differentials into macroscopic circulation and flux statements and explain wave propagation. Retarded potentials solve the wave equation with causality at the speed of light; Liénard–Wiechert fields describe arbitrary sources. In the wave zone, multipole expansion gives dipole radiation and the Larmor formula. Energy balance forces the Abraham–Lorentz radiation-reaction term, tying dissipated power to the jerk.

General relativity and curved spacetime

Gravity is identified with the geometry of a dynamical manifold. The metric tensor \(g_{ik}\) generalizes the flat Minkowski metric; general covariance demands tensor equations. Covariant derivatives and the Levi-Civita connection are fixed by metric compatibility and vanishing torsion. The Riemann tensor, from the commutator of covariant derivatives, measures curvature via parallel transport. The Einstein–Hilbert action, varied with respect to the metric, yields the Einstein equations linking curvature to the energy–momentum tensor. The Schwarzschild solution is the spherically symmetric vacuum; geodesic motion supplies the effective potential including the relativistic perihelion precession.

Global and topological closure

The local calculus lifts to exterior forms: the potential is a 1-form, field strength \(F = \mathrm{d}A\), and Maxwell’s equations become \(\mathrm{d}F = 0\) and \(\mathrm{d}\star F = \star J\) with the Hodge star \(\star\) in the Lorentzian metric. de Rham cohomology encodes global obstructions to potentials on nontrivial topology. Linearized gravity gives gravitational waves in transverse-traceless gauge, with radiated power tied to the third time derivative of the mass quadrupole. The cosmological principle restricts the metric to FLRW form; Friedmann equations govern the scale factor and imply an initial singularity where curvature and density diverge — the geometric picture of the hot big bang.
