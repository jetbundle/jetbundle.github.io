mathematical physics

a structural companion to classical physics — the forced mathematical hierarchy

while the classical spine is laid out in basics—from variational principles through symmetries, reduction, linearization, phase space, special relativity, waves and radiation, general relativity, and global topological closure—this page enumerates the mathematical methods that make those structures possible. the work of mathematics is to characterize problems and their underlying shape with precision; physics poses the constraints, and the minimal inevitable language follows. nothing here is ornament: each object is the smallest structure compatible with what basics already forces. below is rigorous where it matters, still wired back to the classical text at every step.

algebraic substrate and categorical foundations

the program in variational calculus and symmetries treats spatial homogeneity, isotropy, and gauge redundancies as constraints on allowed lagrangians and on admissible changes of description. to state those constraints without contradiction we need composition, inversion, comparison, and linear superposition, each governed by axioms that close under the operations physics actually uses. the layer below is that minimal closure, not a parallel story.

from monoids to groups

a monoid is a set \(M\) with an associative binary operation \(\cdot:M\times M\to M\) and a two-sided unit \(e\in M\): \(a\cdot(b\cdot c)=(a\cdot b)\cdot c\) and \(e\cdot a=a\cdot e=a\). many composed processes are monoids. symmetries of a dynamical system are modeled as invertible transformations of state space: each admissible change of configuration must admit a physical inverse.

a group is a set \(G\) with \(\cdot:G\times G\to G\) satisfying

  1. associativity: \((g_1\cdot g_2)\cdot g_3=g_1\cdot(g_2\cdot g_3)\) for all \(g_1,g_2,g_3\in G\);
  2. identity: there exists \(e\in G\) with \(g\cdot e=e\cdot g=g\) for all \(g\in G\);
  3. inverses: for each \(g\in G\) there exists \(g^{-1}\in G\) with \(g\cdot g^{-1}=g^{-1}\cdot g=e\).

from these axioms alone, identity and inverses are unique (if \(e,e'\) are units then \(e=e\cdot e'=e'\); if \(h,h'\) are both inverses of \(g\) then \(h=h\cdot e=h\cdot(g\cdot h')=(h\cdot g)\cdot h'=e\cdot h'=h'\)).

theorem: admissible symmetries form a group

fix a set \(X\) of states (configurations, phase-space points, fields on a slice—see basics throughout). suppose “symmetry” means a bijection \(f:X\to X\) that preserves whatever dynamical structure has been fixed: e.g. carries solutions of the equations of motion to solutions, or preserves an action on admissible paths. let \(G\) be the set of such bijections closed under the operations used in the theory.

if \(f,g\in G\), then \(f\circ g\) is again a bijection preserving the same structure. associativity is associativity of composition. \(\mathrm{id}_X\in G\). physical reversibility requires that whenever \(f\in G\), the inverse map \(f^{-1}\) is again admissible, hence in \(G\). therefore \((G,\circ)\) is a group. in practice one often replaces \(G\) by a subgroup—for example the connected component of the identity in a lie group of transformations—or quotients by a normal subgroup of redundancies; that matches orbit reduction in reduction and effective potentials.

subgroups, homomorphisms, normal subgroups, quotients

a subgroup \(H\subseteq G\) is a nonempty subset such that \(ab\in H\) whenever \(a,b\in H\) and \(a^{-1}\in H\) whenever \(a\in H\) (equivalently \(ab^{-1}\in H\) whenever \(a,b\in H\)). the intersection of any family of subgroups is a subgroup. a subset \(S\subseteq G\) generates \(G\) if every element of \(G\) is a finite product of elements of \(S\) and their inverses.

a map \(\varphi:G\to G'\) is a homomorphism if \(\varphi(g_1g_2)=\varphi(g_1)\varphi(g_2)\) for all \(g_1,g_2\in G\). then \(\ker\varphi=\{g\in G:\varphi(g)=e'\}\) is a subgroup of \(G\), and \(\mathrm{im}\,\varphi\) is a subgroup of \(G'\).

a subgroup \(N\subseteq G\) is normal (\(N\trianglelefteq G\)) if \(gng^{-1}\in N\) for all \(g\in G\) and \(n\in N\). then the coset space \(G/N=\{gN:g\in G\}\) inherits a well-defined product \((aN)(bN)=(ab)N\), the quotient map \(G\to G/N\) is a homomorphism with kernel \(N\), and the first isomorphism theorem identifies \(G/\ker\varphi\) with \(\mathrm{im}\,\varphi\). normal subgroups are the algebraic face of constraints and redundancies one may quotient without breaking the remaining dynamics—dual, in the classical narrative, to factoring gauge and passing to true degrees of freedom.

categories, functors, exactness

a category \(\mathcal{C}\) has objects \(\mathrm{Ob}(\mathcal{C})\), morphism sets \(\mathrm{Hom}(X,Y)\) for each pair of objects, associative composition \(\circ:\mathrm{Hom}(Y,Z)\times\mathrm{Hom}(X,Y)\to\mathrm{Hom}(X,Z)\), and identities \(1_X\in\mathrm{Hom}(X,X)\). configuration spaces, spacetimes, and phase spaces are objects; once a structure is fixed (smooth, symplectic, lorentzian), the morphisms are the maps preserving that structure.

a functor \(F:\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{D}\) maps objects and morphisms, preserves identities, and satisfies \(F(g\circ f)=F(g)\circ F(f)\) (covariant case). a contravariant functor reverses composition: \(F(g\circ f)=F(f)\circ F(g)\). functors formalize “change of description” that remains coherent under chaining—essential once fields live on bundles and laws are stated functorially.

in an abelian category (vector spaces, sheaves of modules, …), a sequence \(A\xrightarrow{f} B\xrightarrow{g}C\) is exact at \(B\) if \(\mathrm{im}\,f=\ker g\). longer exact sequences track how constraints and quotients propagate information—algebraic shadow of the same conservation and reduction themes that noether’s theorem and gauge fixing express on the classical side.

direct products and direct sums are pinned down by universal properties: they are unique up to isomorphism as the objects that mediate all compatible families of morphisms into or out of the factors. that is the correct notion of combining or decomposing independent subsystems at the level of structure.

vector spaces, linear maps, spectra, jordan form

a vector space over a field \(\mathbb{F}\) (\(\mathbb{R}\) or \(\mathbb{C}\) here) is an abelian group \((V,+)\) with a scalar multiplication \(\mathbb{F}\times V\to V\) satisfying the usual distributive and associative laws with the field. vectors \(v_1,\ldots,v_n\) are linearly dependent if \(\sum_i a_i v_i=0\) for some scalars \(a_i\), not all zero. a basis is a linearly independent spanning set; any two bases have the same cardinality \(\dim V\).

a map \(T:V\to W\) is linear if \(T(au+bv)=aT(u)+bT(v)\). after bases are chosen, \(T\) is a matrix; change of basis conjugates that matrix. \(\lambda\in\mathbb{F}\) is an eigenvalue if \(Tv=\lambda v\) for some \(v\neq 0\). over \(\mathbb{C}\), every operator on finite-dimensional \(V\) has an eigenvalue. over \(\mathbb{R}\), real symmetric operators—self-adjoint for the dot product—are orthogonally diagonalizable (spectral theorem).

over the algebraic closure, every linear operator on finite-dimensional \(V\) admits a jordan normal form: a basis in which \(T\) is block-diagonal with blocks \(J_m(\lambda)=\lambda I_m+N_m\), where \(N_m\) is nilpotent with ones on the first superdiagonal. jordan blocks package generalized eigenspaces; nondiagonalizable pieces often mark degenerate or resonant sectors in linearized dynamics, as in linearization and normal modes.

example: inertia tensor as a symmetric operator on \(\mathbb{R}^3\)

in basics, rigid-body kinetic energy is \(T=\tfrac12\sum_{i,k}I_{ik}\Omega_i\Omega_k\) with \(I_{ik}=I_{ki}\). thus \(I\) defines a symmetric linear operator on \(\mathbb{R}^3\). the spectral theorem gives an orthonormal basis of principal axes diagonalizing \(I\) with eigenvalues \(I_1,I_2,I_3\). this is not a separate trick from linear algebra: the physical tensor is exactly in the class forced to be orthogonally diagonalizable. contrast with a general non-symmetric operator, where jordan form (or complex triangularization) replaces principal-axis diagonalization.

reading the dictionary without a table

lie groups are groups with compatible smooth structure; they house continuous families of symmetries (rotations, boosts, translations) used everywhere in basics. the associated lie algebra infinitesimalizes the group and carries brackets, moment maps, and conserved quantities. normal subgroups and quotients align with factoring gauge and identifying orbits. linear spaces and operators underpin normal modes, stability, and the inertia construction. categories and functors organize covariant and contravariant laws under change of variables and over bundles. next, metric and multilinear structures add inner products, indefinite metrics, and symplectic forms—the tensors that encode measurement and canonical geometry on top of this substrate.

metric and multilinear structures

once states live in vector spaces (superposition, small oscillations), “how large” and “which directions are orthogonal” are extra data. the classical spine uses that data repeatedly: kinetic energy as a positive-definite quadratic form, metric intervals on spacetime, and the symplectic pairing on phase space. this section fixes the definitions so those uses in basics are instances of a single pattern.

bilinear forms, signature, nondegeneracy

a bilinear form on a real vector space \(V\) is a map \(g:V\times V\to\mathbb{R}\) linear in each argument separately. it is symmetric if \(g(u,v)=g(v,u)\), skew-symmetric if \(g(u,v)=-g(v,u)\). in a basis \(\{e_i\}\), \(g_{ij}=g(e_i,e_j)\) is the matrix of \(g\); for \(u=\sum u^i e_i\), \(v=\sum v^j e_j\), \(g(u,v)=\sum_{i,j} g_{ij}u^i v^j\). \(g\) is nondegenerate if \(g(u,v)=0\) for all \(v\) implies \(u=0\) (equivalently \(\det(g_{ij})\neq 0\) in some, hence every, basis).

for symmetric nondegenerate \(g\) over \(\mathbb{R}\), sylvester’s law of inertia says there is a basis in which \(g_{ij}\) is diagonal with entries \(\pm 1\); the count \((p,q)\) of plus and minus signs is the signature, a basis invariant. positive definite (\(p=\dim V\), \(q=0\)) is euclidean geometry: lengths \(\|v\|=\sqrt{g(v,v)}\), angles from \(g\), and gram–schmidt orthogonalization. lorentzian signature \((1,3)\) or \((3,1)\) is the linear model of spacetime intervals and light cones in special relativity.

symplectic vector spaces

a symplectic form on finite-dimensional \(V\) is a skew-symmetric, nondegenerate bilinear map \(\omega:V\times V\to\mathbb{R}\). then \(\dim V\) is even, and there exists a darboux basis in which \(\omega\) has block matrix \(\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}0&I\\-I&0\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)\). the top form \(\omega^n/n!\) is a canonical volume. this is the linear prototype of phase space in phase space and canonical structure: momenta pair with coordinates through \(\omega\), not through a metric.

tensors and the exterior algebra

the tensor product \(V\otimes W\) (finite-dimensional) is characterized by bilinearity of \((v,w)\mapsto v\otimes w\) and universal factorization of every bilinear map \(V\times W\to U\). multilinear maps \(V^k\to\mathbb{R}\) identify with \((V^*)^{\otimes k}\). symmetrization and alternation project to subspaces carrying the symmetric group action; the alternating part yields the exterior powers \(\Lambda^k V^*\), generated by wedge products with \(\alpha\wedge\beta=(-1)^{pq}\beta\wedge\alpha\) for \(\alpha\in\Lambda^p\), \(\beta\in\Lambda^q\). that is the coordinate-free home of antisymmetric field strengths and volume elements used in forms on manifolds.

differentiable manifolds and the calculus of forms

configuration space and spacetime are rarely one global coordinate patch. general relativity and maxwell on manifolds require a definition of “smooth structure” that glues local euclidean models consistently.

charts, atlases, tangent and cotangent bundles

a topological \(n\)-manifold is a second-countable hausdorff space locally homeomorphic to \(\mathbb{R}^n\). a \(C^k\) atlas is a family of pairs \((U_\alpha,\varphi_\alpha)\) with \(U_\alpha\) open, \(\bigcup_\alpha U_\alpha=M\), and \(\varphi_\alpha:U_\alpha\to\mathbb{R}^n\) homeomorphisms onto open sets, such that transition maps \(\varphi_\beta\circ\varphi_\alpha^{-1}\) are \(C^k\) on overlaps. a \(C^k\) manifold is an equivalence class of compatible atlases.

at \(p\in M\), tangent vectors may be defined as equivalence classes of curves through \(p\) (same first derivative in one, hence every, chart) or as derivations of germs of \(C^k\) functions at \(p\). they form a vector space \(T_pM\) of dimension \(n\). the disjoint union \(TM=\bigsqcup_{p\in M}T_pM\) is the tangent bundle; with the natural manifold charts it is a \(C^{k-1}\) vector bundle over \(M\). the dual bundle \(T^*M\) has fibers \(T^*_pM=(T_pM)^*\); sections are one-forms.

on a smooth manifold, partitions of unity subordinate to any open cover let one patch local constructions—metrics, connections, integrals—into global objects. that is the analytic reason global statements in basics are legitimate once stated chart-invariantly.

differential forms and the exterior derivative

a differential \(k\)-form is a smooth section of \(\Lambda^k T^*M\). in local coordinates, \(\alpha=\sum_{i_1<\cdotsexterior derivative \(\mathrm{d}\) is the unique \(\mathbb{R}\)-linear map on forms raising degree by one, satisfying the leibniz rule \(\mathrm{d}(\alpha\wedge\beta)=\mathrm{d}\alpha\wedge\beta+ (-1)^k\alpha\wedge\mathrm{d}\beta\) for \(\alpha\) a \(k\)-form, and \(\mathrm{d}^2=0\). on \(\mathbb{R}^3\), \(\mathrm{d}\) encodes gradient, curl, and divergence in one operator. the identity \(\mathrm{d}^2=0\) is the source of conserved, cohomological content: closed forms need not be exact globally.

the lie derivative \(\mathcal{L}_X\) along a vector field \(X\) measures how tensors flow with the flow of \(X\); cartan’s magic formula \(\mathcal{L}_X=\mathrm{d}\,\iota_X+\iota_X\,\mathrm{d}\) links it to contraction \(\iota_X\) and \(\mathrm{d}\). frobenius’ theorem characterizes when a distribution of subspaces is tangent to foliations—i.e. when constraints are holonomic. together these tools are the differential-geometric backbone of symmetry reduction and constrained mechanics on manifolds.

cohomological invariants and spectral topology

the distinction “closed form” versus “globally exact form” in global and topological closure is not pedantry: it carries fluxes, winding numbers, and obstructions to choosing global gauges. de rham cohomology is the functorial invariant that counts those phenomena.

de rham cohomology

let \(M\) be a smooth manifold. write \(\Omega^k(M)\) for smooth \(k\)-forms. \(\mathrm{d}:\Omega^k(M)\to \Omega^{k+1}(M)\) satisfies \(\mathrm{d}^2=0\). a form \(\omega\) is closed if \(\mathrm{d}\omega=0\), exact if \(\omega=\mathrm{d}\eta\) for some \(\eta\). every exact form is closed. the \(k\)-th de rham cohomology is the quotient vector space

\[ H^k_{\mathrm{dR}}(M)=\frac{\{\omega\in\Omega^k(M):\mathrm{d}\omega=0\}}{\{\omega=\mathrm{d}\eta\}}. \]

its dimension \(b_k=\dim H^k_{\mathrm{dR}}(M)\) is the \(k\)-th betti number (for reasonable \(M\), finite in each degree). pullback along smooth maps \(f:M\to N\) induces \(f^*:H^k_{\mathrm{dR}}(N)\to H^k_{\mathrm{dR}}(M)\), functorially.

poincaré lemma: on a contractible open set, every closed form is exact. failure of contractibility produces the global obstructions the classical text names when discussing monopoles, dirac strings, and nontrivial bundles.

computations, duality, bundles

mayer–vietoris and čech–de rham machinery compute \(H^*_{\mathrm{dR}}(M)\) from covers—exact sequences relate cohomology of pieces, intersections, and the union. on a closed oriented \(n\)-manifold, poincaré duality gives a nondegenerate pairing \(H^k_{\mathrm{dR}}(M)\times H^{n-k}_{\mathrm{dR}}(M)\to\mathbb{R}\), identifying degrees that carry complementary geometric data (sources versus fields, cycles versus fluxes).

for vector bundles \(E\to M\), characteristic classes such as euler and thom classes live in cohomology of \(M\) or of the total space and detect obstructions to nonvanishing sections (e.g. hairy ball phenomena) and twisting of bundles. spectral sequences organize filtered complexes arising from fibrations and local coefficient systems. these are the systematic language behind the “topological closure” paragraphs in basics.

the kinematics and dynamics of classical mechanics

this section names the geometric objects that the classical narrative in basics manipulates: configuration manifolds, lagrangians on the tangent bundle, legendre transforms to the cotangent bundle, and symplectic hamiltonian flows.

configuration space and the tangent bundle

let \(Q\) be a smooth manifold of generalized coordinates \(q^i\) (possibly with constraints already embedded, so \(Q\) is the reduced configuration space). the tangent bundle \(TQ\) has points \((q,v)\) with \(v\in T_qQ\). a lagrangian is a function \(L:TQ\to\mathbb{R}\) (possibly time dependent); the action on a path \(q(t)\) is \(S[q]=\int L(q,\dot{q},t)\,dt\). stationarity of \(S\) under fixed-endpoint variations yields the euler–lagrange equations in coordinates: \(\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial\dot{q}^i}-\frac{\partial L}{\partial q^i}=0\). this is the coordinate-free content of variational calculus, expressed invariantly as an equation on \(TQ\) or, via the variational principle, on the path space of maps into \(Q\).

legendre transform and the symplectic cotangent bundle

when the fiber hessian \(\partial^2 L/\partial\dot{q}^i\partial\dot{q}^j\) is nondegenerate, the legendre map \(\mathbb{F}L:TQ\to T^*Q\) sends \((q,\dot{q})\mapsto(q,p)\) with \(p_i=\partial L/\partial\dot{q}^i\). the hamiltonian on \(T^*Q\) is \(H=p_i\dot{q}^i-L\) expressed as a function of \((q,p)\). the canonical one-form \(\theta=p_i\,dq^i\) on \(T^*Q\) and the symplectic form \(\omega=-\mathrm{d}\theta=\sum_i dq^i\wedge dp_i\) (in darboux coordinates) make \((T^*Q,\omega)\) a symplectic manifold. hamilton’s equations are the flow of the vector field \(X_H\) defined by \(\iota_{X_H}\omega=\mathrm{d}H\). equivalently, the poisson bracket \(\{f,g\}=\omega(X_f,X_g)\) encodes the same dynamics. this is the geometric content of phase space and canonical structure, linked also to symplectic linear algebra above.

symmetries, constants, integrable structure

a lie group action on \(Q\) or \(TQ\) leaving \(L\) invariant generates conserved quantities via noether’s theorem (made precise via momentum maps in symplectic geometry). hamilton–jacobi theory seeks a generating function \(S(q,t)\) solving \(H(q,\partial S/\partial q)+\partial S/\partial t=0\), rebuilding families of trajectories from a single scalar. in integrable systems, action–angle coordinates linearize flow on invariant tori; adiabatic invariants track approximate conservation of actions under slow parameter change—topics treated concretely in the same classical sections. global issues (e.g. monodromy, nontrivial \(TQ\) or \(T^*Q\) topology) refine the picture but the local hamiltonian formalism above is the universal core.

relativistic spacetime and electrodynamics

the classical development in special relativity, waves and radiation, and maxwell as forms assumes a lorentzian background and gauge fields as connections; here is the compact mathematical packaging.

minkowski space and the lorentz group

choose units with \(c=1\). minkowski space is \(\mathbb{R}^4\) with metric \(\eta=\mathrm{diag} (-1,1,1,1)\) and line element \(ds^2=\eta_{\mu\nu}\,dx^\mu dx^\nu\). a linear map \(\Lambda\) is a lorentz transformation if \(\Lambda^T\eta\Lambda=\eta\). they form the indefinite orthogonal group \(O(1,3)\); the component preserving orientation and time orientation is often written \(SO^+(1,3)\). four-vectors \(V^\mu\) transform by \(V'^\mu=\Lambda^\mu{}_\nu V^\nu\); covectors use \(\eta_{\mu\nu}\) to lower indices. causal structure: vectors with \(\eta(V,V)<0\) are timelike; null vectors define light cones.

maxwell as gauge theory on a lorentzian manifold

on a general lorentzian four-manifold \((M,g)\), the electromagnetic potential is a one-form \(A\); the field strength is the exact two-form \(F=\mathrm{d}A\) (so \(\mathrm{d}F=0\) is the homogeneous maxwell system, bianchi identity). sources enter through the hodge star \(\star_g\) built from \(g\): the inhomogeneous equation is \(\mathrm{d}\star_g F=\star_g J\) for current three-form \(J\). on minkowski space in cartesian coordinates this reproduces the standard \(\partial_\mu F^{\mu\nu}=J^\nu\) after identifying components. gauge freedom \(A\mapsto A+\mathrm{d}\chi\) leaves \(F\) unchanged—precisely a one-form worth of redundancy quotiented in the classical discussion of potentials.

the stress–energy tensor \(T^{\mu\nu}\) packages local energy, momentum, and stress; its divergence vanishes when fields obey the coupled equations of motion (matter plus maxwell), encoding conservation compatible with lorentz symmetry. retarded potentials and liénard–wiechert fields are explicit green-function solutions tied to causal support of sources on lorentzian manifolds—the same causality enforced geometrically by the metric cone structure.

geometric gravity, connections, and curvature

general relativity and curved spacetime identifies the gravitational field with a lorentzian metric \(g\) on a four-manifold \(M\). the mathematical layer spells out how \(g\) fixes geometry, curvature, and field equations.

levi-civita connection and curvature

a metric on \(M\) is a smooth symmetric nondegenerate two-tensor \(g\) of lorentzian signature. the levi-civita connection \(\nabla\) is the unique affine connection that is metric compatible (\(\nabla g=0\)) and torsion-free. in coordinates, christoffel symbols \(\Gamma^k_{ij}=\tfrac12 g^{k\ell}(\partial_i g_{j\ell}+\partial_j g_{i\ell}-\partial_\ell g_{ij})\) define covariant derivatives \(\nabla_i V^k=\partial_i V^k+\Gamma^k_{ij}V^j\).

the riemann curvature tensor \(R^\rho{}_{\sigma\mu\nu}\) measures failure of covariant derivatives to commute and encodes holonomy of parallel transport around infinitesimal loops. the ricci tensor is the contraction \(R_{\mu\nu}=R^\rho{}_{\mu\rho\nu}\); the ricci scalar is \(R=g^{\mu\nu}R_{\mu\nu}\). the einstein tensor \(G_{\mu\nu}=R_{\mu\nu}-\tfrac12 R\,g_{\mu\nu}\) satisfies the contracted bianchi identity \(\nabla_\mu G^{\mu\nu}=0\), parallel to conservation of stress–energy.

einstein’s equations and vacuum solutions

the einstein–hilbert action (with cosmological constant \(\Lambda\) optional) is \(S=\frac{1}{16\pi G}\int_M (R-2\Lambda)\,\mathrm{vol}_g+\int_M \mathcal{L}_m\,\mathrm{vol}_g\). variation with respect to \(g\) yields einstein’s equations \(G_{\mu\nu}+\Lambda g_{\mu\nu}=8\pi G\,T_{\mu\nu}\), where \(T_{\mu\nu}\) is the matter stress–energy tensor. vacuum solutions (\(T_{\mu\nu}=0\)) include schwarzschild, the unique static, spherically symmetric, asymptotically flat black hole geometry in four dimensions (birkhoff’s theorem). geodesics of \(g\) are the worldlines of freely falling test particles; deviation of nearby geodesics is controlled by the riemann tensor—source of tidal forces and, in the weak-field limit, the extra \(r^{-3}\) contribution to perihelion precession described in basics.
