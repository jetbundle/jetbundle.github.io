mathematical physics

a structural companion to classical physics — the forced mathematical hierarchy

while the classical spine is laid out in basics—from variational principles through symmetries, reduction, linearization, phase space, special relativity, waves and radiation, general relativity, and global topological closure—this page enumerates the mathematical methods that make those structures possible. the work of mathematics is to characterize problems and their underlying shape with precision; physics poses the constraints, and the minimal inevitable language follows. nothing here is ornament: each object is the smallest structure compatible with what basics already forces. below is rigorous where it matters, still wired back to the classical text at every step.

algebraic substrate and categorical foundations

any physical theory names states that can be compared, composed, and transformed. once those operations are required to be coherent—associative composition, reversibility for symmetries, superposition for small excitations—the minimal algebraic objects are forced: monoids, then groups; vector spaces; and, when several levels of structure must be related consistently, categories and functors. the classical narrative in variational calculus and symmetries already implements this layer; here it is stated so nothing essential stays implicit.

monoid: the bare minimum of composition

a monoid is a pair $(M,\cdot)$ where $M$ is a set and $\cdot:M\times M\to M$ is an associative binary operation with a two-sided unit $e\in M$:

$$ a\cdot(b\cdot c)=(a\cdot b)\cdot c,\qquad e\cdot a=a\cdot e=a\quad\forall a,b,c\in M. $$

many “processes chained in time” are monoids. symmetries of a system, however, are modeled by invertible transformations: every admissible move of state space must be undoable. a general monoid does not demand inverses, so it is too weak for reversible symmetry.

group axioms and elementary consequences

a group is a pair $(G,\cdot)$ with $\cdot:G\times G\to G$ satisfying, for all $g,g_1,g_2,g_3\in G$, the following (numbered as usual in texts):

  1. associativity: $(g_1\cdot g_2)\cdot g_3=g_1\cdot(g_2\cdot g_3)$;
  2. identity: there exists $e\in G$ with $g\cdot e=e\cdot g=g$;
  3. inverses: there exists $g^{-1}\in G$ with $g\cdot g^{-1}=g^{-1}\cdot g=e$.
$$ \begin{aligned} (g_1\cdot g_2)\cdot g_3 &= g_1\cdot(g_2\cdot g_3),\\ \exists\,e\in G:\ g\cdot e=e\cdot g&=g,\\ \forall g\ \exists\,g^{-1}\in G:\ g\cdot g^{-1}=g^{-1}\cdot g&=e. \end{aligned} $$

from (2)–(3) one proves uniqueness of the identity and uniqueness of inverses. if $e,e'$ are both identities, then $e=e\cdot e'=e'$. if $h,h'$ are both inverses of $g$, then

$$ h = h\cdot e = h\cdot(g\cdot h') = (h\cdot g)\cdot h' = e\cdot h' = h'. $$

hence we may speak of the identity $e$ and the inverse $g^{-1}$ without ambiguity.

theorem: symmetries compatible with composition form a group

fix a set $X$ of states (configurations, phase-space points, fields on a fixed slice—throughout basics). fix a distinguished class $\mathcal{S}$ of bijections $f:X\to X$ deemed symmetries: for example, maps that send every solution of a given evolution equation to another solution, or preserve an action functional on admissible histories. assume:

  1. if $f,g\in\mathcal{S}$ then $f\circ g\in\mathcal{S}$ (closure under composition);
  2. $\mathrm{id}_X\in\mathcal{S}$;
  3. if $f\in\mathcal{S}$ then $f^{-1}\in\mathcal{S}$ (reversibility).

then $(\mathcal{S},\circ)$ is a group. write the group data compactly as

$$ (f\circ g)(x)=f(g(x)),\qquad e=\mathrm{id}_X,\qquad f^{-1}\circ f=f\circ f^{-1}=e. $$

associativity is associativity of composition of maps; inverses are set-theoretic inverses of bijections. in applications one often replaces $\mathcal{S}$ by a subgroup (e.g. the identity component of a lie group of transformations) or quotients a normal subgroup of gauge redundancies—precisely the orbit and constraint picture in reduction and effective potentials.

subgroups, generators, homomorphisms

a subset $H\subseteq G$ is a subgroup (written $H\le G$) if $H\neq\emptyset$, $ab\in H$ whenever $a,b\in H$, and $a^{-1}\in H$ whenever $a\in H$ (equivalently $ab^{-1}\in H$ for all $a,b\in H$). the intersection of any nonempty family of subgroups is a subgroup. a subset $S\subseteq G$ generates $G$ if every element of $G$ is a finite product of elements of $S$ and their inverses.

a map $\varphi:G\to G'$ is a homomorphism if it intertwines the products:

$$ \varphi(g_1g_2)=\varphi(g_1)\,\varphi(g_2)\qquad\forall g_1,g_2\in G. $$

its kernel and image are

$$ \ker\varphi=\{g\in G:\varphi(g)=e_{G'}\},\qquad \mathrm{im}\,\varphi=\{\varphi(g):g\in G\}. $$

$\ker\varphi$ is a normal subgroup of $G$ (see below); $\mathrm{im}\,\varphi$ is a subgroup of $G'$. the first isomorphism theorem packages the induced map on cosets as an isomorphism

$$ \bar{\varphi}:\ G/\ker\varphi \xrightarrow{\;\sim\;} \mathrm{im}\,\varphi,\qquad \bar{\varphi}(g\,\ker\varphi)=\varphi(g). $$

normal subgroups, quotients, and gauge-type reduction

a subgroup $N\le G$ is normal, written $N\trianglelefteq G$, if $gng^{-1}\in N$ for all $g\in G$ and $n\in N$ (equivalently $gNg^{-1}=N$ for all $g\in G$). normality is exactly the condition needed for the set of left cosets

$$ G/N = \{gN : g\in G\} $$

to inherit a product on cosets one defines

$$ (aN)\cdot(bN) := (ab)N. $$

this is well-defined if replacing $a\mapsto an$, $b\mapsto bn'$ ($n,n'\in N$) leaves the coset unchanged: one checks $(an)(bn')N=a\bigl(n(bn')n^{-1}\bigr)bN=abN$ using normality to absorb conjugates of $N$ into $N$. then $\pi:G\to G/N$, $\pi(g)=gN$, is a surjective homomorphism with $\ker\pi=N$.

when a redundancy acts on states but does not change physical predictions, quotienting by that normal subgroup implements “the same physics with fewer redundant labels”—the algebraic form of imposing gauge constraints and passing to true degrees of freedom.

categories: objects, morphisms, composition

a category $\mathcal{C}$ consists of:

  1. a class $\mathrm{Ob}(\mathcal{C})$ of objects (configuration spaces, spacetimes, phase spaces, vector bundles, …);
  2. for each ordered pair $(X,Y)$ of objects, a set $\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(X,Y)$ of morphisms;
  3. for each composable triple $(X,Y,Z)$, an associative composition (displayed below);
  4. for each object $X$, an identity morphism $1_X\in\mathrm{Hom}(X,X)$ such that $f\circ 1_X=f$ and $1_Y\circ f=f$ for every $f:X\to Y$.
$$ \circ:\mathrm{Hom}(Y,Z)\times\mathrm{Hom}(X,Y)\to\mathrm{Hom}(X,Z),\qquad (g,f)\mapsto g\circ f, $$

with associativity $(h\circ g)\circ f=h\circ(g\circ f)$ whenever $f:X\to Y$, $g:Y\to Z$, and $h:Z\to W$. identities satisfy, for every $f:X\to Y$,

$$ f\circ 1_X = f,\qquad 1_Y\circ f = f. $$

once a structure is fixed on objects (smooth, symplectic, lorentzian, …), morphisms are precisely the maps preserving that structure—so functorial change of description cannot break the laws encoded in morphisms.

functors and contravariance

a covariant functor $F:\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{D}$ assigns objects and morphisms and preserves identities and composition:

$$ F(1_X)=1_{F(X)},\qquad F(g\circ f)=F(g)\circ F(f)\quad\text{(covariant).} $$

a contravariant functor reverses composition:

$$ F(1_X)=1_{F(X)},\qquad F(g\circ f)=F(f)\circ F(g)\quad\text{(contravariant).} $$

pulling back differential forms along smooth maps is contravariant; pushing forward tangent vectors is covariant.

exactness (abelian setting) and universal properties

in an abelian category (e.g. vector spaces over a field, modules over a ring, sheaves of such modules), a sequence of morphisms

$$ A \xrightarrow{f} B \xrightarrow{g} C $$

is exact at $B$ if the two subobjects coincide:

$$ \mathrm{im}\,f=\ker g\ \subseteq\ B. $$

longer exact sequences organize how constraints, quotients, and extensions fit together—an algebraic shadow of conservation laws, reduction, and gauge fixing on the classical side.

binary products $A\times B$ and coproducts $A\oplus B$ (direct sum in linear categories) are characterized by universal properties: every compatible pair of morphisms into $A$ and $B$ factors uniquely through $A\times B$, and every pair out of $A$ and $B$ factors uniquely through the coproduct. that is the correct abstract notion of combining or splitting independent subsystems at the level of structure, without choosing ad hoc coordinates.

vector space axioms

superposition of small disturbances forces a vector space over a field $\mathbb{F}$ (here $\mathbb{R}$ or $\mathbb{C}$): an abelian group $(V,+)$ together with scalar multiplication $\mathbb{F}\times V\to V$, written $(a,v)\mapsto av$, satisfying for all $u,v\in V$ and $a,b\in\mathbb{F}$:

  1. $u+v=v+u$ (commutativity of $+$);
  2. $(u+v)+w=u+(v+w)$ (associativity of $+$);
  3. there exists $0\in V$ with $v+0=v$ for all $v$;
  4. for each $v$ there exists $-v\in V$ with $v+(-v)=0$;
  5. $a(bv)=(ab)v$ (associativity of scalar action);
  6. $1v=v$ for the multiplicative unit $1\in\mathbb{F}$;
  7. $a(u+v)=au+av$ (distributivity over vector addition);
  8. $(a+b)v=av+bv$ (distributivity over scalar addition).

dependence and dimension are the bookkeeping of superposition:

$$ \sum_{i=1}^n a_i v_i = 0 \ \text{with not all } a_i=0 \quad\Rightarrow\quad \{v_i\}\ \text{dependent};\qquad \dim V = |\text{basis}|. $$

a basis is a maximal linearly independent (equivalently, spanning) set; any two bases of a fixed $V$ have the same cardinality.

linear maps, characteristic polynomial, and Jordan normal form

a map $T:V\to W$ between vector spaces over $\mathbb{F}$ is linear if it respects linear combinations:

$$ T(au+bv)=a\,T(u)+b\,T(v)\qquad\forall a,b\in\mathbb{F},\ u,v\in V. $$

fix a finite basis; $T$ becomes an $n\times n$ matrix $A$. a change of basis replaces $A$ by a conjugate $P^{-1}AP$—the same operator in new coordinates.

$\lambda\in\mathbb{F}$ is an eigenvalue of $T:V\to V$ if

$$ Tv=\lambda v\quad\text{for some }v\neq 0\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \ker(T-\lambda I)\neq\{0\}. $$

the characteristic polynomial packages all eigenvalues (with algebraic multiplicity):

$$ c_T(\lambda):=\det(\lambda I-A). $$

in an algebraic closure of $\mathbb{F}$, $c_T$ factors as $\prod_{j}(\lambda-\lambda_j)^{m_a(\lambda_j)}$ with distinct $\lambda_j$; the exponent $m_a(\lambda_j)$ is the algebraic multiplicity. the geometric multiplicity is $m_g(\lambda):=\dim\ker(T-\lambda I)$. always $1\le m_g(\lambda)\le m_a(\lambda)$. equality $m_g=m_a$ for every eigenvalue holds if and only if $T$ is diagonalizable over the splitting field of $c_T$. over $\mathbb{R}$, a real symmetric matrix is orthogonally diagonalizable (spectral theorem)—the situation for the inertia operator in the next subsection.

jordan normal form: existence, blocks, and dynamics. when $m_g(\lambda)<m_a(\lambda)$ for some eigenvalue $\lambda$, the operator $T-\lambda I$ is not zero on the whole $\lambda$-generalized subspace; eigenvectors alone do not span it. over an algebraically closed field (e.g. $\mathbb{C}$), the jordan canonical form theorem asserts: there exists a basis of $V$ in which the matrix of $T$ is block diagonal,

$$ [T] = \mathrm{diag}\bigl(J_{m_1}(\lambda_1),\,J_{m_2}(\lambda_2),\,\ldots\bigr), $$

each jordan block has the shape

$$ J_m(\lambda)=\lambda I_m+N_m,\qquad (N_m)_{ij}=\begin{cases}1&\text{if }j=i+1,\\0&\text{otherwise.}\end{cases} $$

equivalently, a length-$m$ jordan chain for $\lambda$ is a tuple $(v_1,\ldots,v_m)$ with $(T-\lambda I)v_1=0$ and $(T-\lambda I)v_{j}=v_{j-1}$ for $j\ge 2$; in that basis the block is $J_m(\lambda)$. the matrix $N_m$ is strictly upper triangular with ones on the first superdiagonal; it is nilpotent of index $m$: $N_m^m=0$, while $N_m^{m-1}\neq 0$ when $m\ge 2$ (for $m=1$, $N_1=0$). on $G_\lambda$, writing $T|_{G_\lambda}=\lambda I+N$ with $N$ nilpotent, $N$ and $\lambda I$ commute, so

$$ e^{tJ_m(\lambda)} = e^{t\lambda I}\,e^{tN_m} = e^{\lambda t}\sum_{k=0}^{m-1}\frac{(tN_m)^k}{k!}. $$

the sum stops at $k=m-1$ because $N_m^m=0$. this matrix is the fundamental solution of $\dot x=J_m(\lambda)x$ on $\mathbb{F}^m$.

calculation ($m=2$). $N=\left(\begin{smallmatrix}0&1\\0&0\end{smallmatrix}\right)$, $N^2=0$, hence $e^{tN}=I+tN$ and

$$ e^{tJ_2(\lambda)} = e^{\lambda t}\begin{pmatrix}1&t\\0&1\end{pmatrix} =\begin{pmatrix}e^{\lambda t}&t\,e^{\lambda t}\\0&e^{\lambda t}\end{pmatrix}. $$

for $x(0)=\binom{c_0}{c_1}$,

$$ x(t)=e^{tJ_2(\lambda)}x(0)=\begin{pmatrix}e^{\lambda t}(c_0+t c_1)\\ e^{\lambda t}c_1\end{pmatrix}. $$

if $c_1\neq 0$, the first component is $e^{\lambda t}(c_0+t c_1)$—a secular term $t\,e^{\lambda t}$ alongside $e^{\lambda t}$.

calculation ($m=3$). for the $3\times 3$ block, $N^3=0$ but $N^2\neq 0$, so

$$ e^{tN}=I+tN+\frac{t^2}{2}N^2,\qquad e^{tJ_3(\lambda)}=e^{\lambda t}\begin{pmatrix}1&t&t^2/2\\0&1&t\\0&0&1\end{pmatrix}. $$

thus coordinates along the chain pick up polynomial prefactors up to degree $m-1$ multiplying $e^{\lambda t}$. in discrete time $x_{k+1}=Ax$, the same nilpotent polynomial structure appears in $k$ (via $(A-\lambda I)^r$ on generalized eigenvectors). pure exponentials $e^{\lambda t}$ alone parametrize only $\ker(T-\lambda I)$, not the full $G_\lambda$ when $m_a>m_g$.

generalized eigenspace. for each eigenvalue $\lambda$ of $T$ (root of $c_T$), define

$$ G_\lambda:=\ker(T-\lambda I)^{m_a(\lambda)}. $$

over $\mathbb{C}$, $V=\bigoplus_\lambda G_\lambda$ (primary decomposition), each $G_\lambda$ is $T$-invariant, $\dim G_\lambda=m_a(\lambda)$, and $T|_{G_\lambda}$ is similar to a direct sum of jordan blocks $J_{m_i}(\lambda)$ whose sizes $m_i$ sum to $m_a(\lambda)$. the number of blocks for $\lambda$ equals $m_g(\lambda)$; the largest block size is the exponent of $(x-\lambda)$ in the minimal polynomial of $T$. jordan form is therefore the canonical coordinate system in which $T$ is a direct sum of indecomposable commuting pieces “scalar $+$ strictly upper shift”; computing $e^{tT}$ reduces to the block formula above.

in linearized dynamics around equilibria or periodic orbits, $T$ is typically the Jacobian linearization; defective eigenvalues produce the secular terms that pure normal-mode analysis misses—see linearization and normal modes.

example: inertia tensor versus Jordan form

work in a body-fixed orthonormal frame so the angular velocity is $\Omega=(\Omega_1,\Omega_2,\Omega_3)^T\in\mathbb{R}^3$. as in basics, the rotational kinetic energy is the quadratic form

$$ T=\frac12\sum_{i,k} I_{ik}\Omega_i\Omega_k=\frac12\,\Omega^{\mathsf T}I\Omega,\qquad I_{ik}=I_{ki}\in\mathbb{R}. $$

thus $I$ is a concrete $3\times 3$ real symmetric matrix. identify it with $I:\mathbb{R}^3\to\mathbb{R}^3$, $v\mapsto Iv$. with the standard inner product $\langle u,v\rangle=u^{\mathsf T}v$, self-adjointness is the matrix identity

$$ \langle Iu,v\rangle = u^{\mathsf T}I^{\mathsf T}v = u^{\mathsf T}Iv = \langle u,Iv\rangle \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad I^{\mathsf T}=I. $$

the spectral theorem for such operators asserts the existence of $Q\in O(3)$ with

$$ Q^{\mathsf T}IQ=\mathrm{diag}(I_1,I_2,I_3),\qquad I_j\in\mathbb{R}, $$

the columns of $Q$ are orthonormal eigenvectors (principal axes fixed in the body); $I_1,I_2,I_3$ are the principal moments. in principal components $\tilde\Omega=Q^{\mathsf T}\Omega$,

$$ T=\frac12\bigl(I_1\tilde\Omega_1^2+I_2\tilde\Omega_2^2+I_3\tilde\Omega_3^2\bigr). $$

for symmetric real $I$, every eigenvalue has $m_g=m_a$, so the jordan form is diagonal—there is nothing further to extract from the jordan–nilpotent picture for this operator; the entire $\lambda$-generalized subspace is $\ker(I-\lambda I)$.

contrast of roles: $I$ is fixed body data; $R(t)\in SO(3)$ is configuration in the rotation group; the euler equations in the body frame are

$$ I\dot\Omega+\Omega\times(I\Omega)=0. $$

linearizing about a steady spin gives a $3\times 3$ real matrix acting on $\delta\Omega$ that need not be symmetric; its eigenvalues govern stability (intermediate-axis instability appears there). that matrix is not $I$; orthogonal diagonalizability of $I$ is logically separate from stability of a particular solution.

for multiplicity, jordan blocks, secular terms $t^k e^{\lambda t}$, and $G_\lambda$, see the preceding subsection; a generic linearization matrix $A$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$ or $\mathbb{C}^n$ need not match the special symmetric–real pattern of $I$.

lie groups, quotients, and how this layer attaches to physics

a lie group is a finite-dimensional smooth manifold $G$ together with smooth structure maps

$$ \mu:G\times G\to G\ \text{(product)},\qquad \iota:G\to G\ \text{(inverse)} $$

such that $(G,\mu,\iota)$ satisfies the usual group axioms (associativity, unit $e$, inverses). matrix groups $GL(n,\mathbb{R})$, $SL(n,\mathbb{R})$, $O(n)$, $SO(n)$, and $O(1,3)\subset GL(4,\mathbb{R})$ are closed subgroups of some $GL(m,\mathbb{R})$ in the subspace topology, hence embedded lie groups (closed subgroup theorem).

the lie algebra is $\mathfrak{g}=T_{e}G$. for matrix groups, one-parameter subgroups are $\gamma_\xi(t)=\exp(t\xi)$ with the convergent series

$$ \exp(A)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{A^n}{n!},\qquad \gamma_\xi(0)=I,\ \dot\gamma_\xi(0)=\xi. $$

the bracket agrees with the matrix commutator on subgroups of $GL(n,\mathbb{R})$:

$$ [\xi,\eta]=\xi\eta-\eta\xi,\qquad \xi,\eta\in\mathfrak{g}\subset\mathfrak{gl}(n,\mathbb{R}). $$

$\exp:\mathfrak{g}\to G$ is a local diffeomorphism near $0$; thus $\exp(\varepsilon\xi)$ is the first-order finite symmetry generated by the infinitesimal direction $\xi$—the usual input to noether- type arguments.

if $H\subseteq G$ is a closed normal lie subgroup, the quotient $G/H$ is a smooth manifold and

$$ \pi:G\to G/H\ \text{ is a submersion and a homomorphism},\qquad \dim(G/H)=\dim G-\dim H. $$

that is the smooth counterpart of the algebraic quotient $G/N$ used for discrete redundancies.

read this alongside the earlier algebraic quotient $G/N$ for abstract groups: for lie groups one adds smoothness so tangent spaces, brackets, and moment maps are available. normal subgroups still encode redundancies quotientable without breaking the remaining symmetry structure; functors between categories of $G$-spaces or principal $G$-bundles formalize how fields and potentials change under $G$.

each earlier object answers a definable requirement: composition and inversion define abstract groups; smooth dependence selects lie groups and $\mathfrak{g}$; linear superposition selects vector spaces; self-adjoint $I$ selects spectral data on $\mathbb{R}^3$; general $A$ in linearizations selects jordan/real canonical form; normal subgroups and $G/H$ select reduced degrees of freedom; morphisms and functors select coherent changes of model. metrics, manifolds, forms, and cohomology in the following sections build on this substrate. next: metric and multilinear structures.

metric and multilinear structures

with vector spaces and linear maps fixed (algebraic substrate), many physical quantities still cannot be stated: magnitude along a direction, orthogonality, invariant intervals, canonical pairing of coordinates and momenta, or dependence on several vectors at once. vector addition and scalar multiplication do not determine those notions. the minimal extensions are bilinear (and sesquilinear) forms, then tensor products and their symmetrized/alternating quotients. what follows makes each object’s definition, axioms, and role in basics explicit—without compressing that map into a schematic summary.

bilinear forms: axioms, matrices, nondegeneracy

let $V$ be finite-dimensional over $\mathbb{R}$. a map $g:V\times V\to\mathbb{R}$ is bilinear if it is linear in each argument when the other is held fixed:

$$ \begin{aligned} g(\alpha u_1+\beta u_2,\,v) &= \alpha\,g(u_1,v)+\beta\,g(u_2,v),\\ g(u,\,\gamma v_1+\delta v_2) &= \gamma\,g(u,v_1)+\delta\,g(u,v_2). \end{aligned} $$

it is symmetric if $g(u,v)=g(v,u)$, skew-symmetric if $g(u,v)=-g(v,u)$. fix a basis $\{e_i\}_{i=1}^n$ and components $u=\sum_i u^i e_i$, $v=\sum_j v^j e_j$. define $g_{ij}:=g(e_i,e_j)$. then

$$ g(u,v)=\sum_{i,j=1}^n g_{ij}\,u^i v^j. $$

$g$ is nondegenerate if the equivalent conditions hold: (i) $g(u,v)=0$ for all $v$ implies $u=0$; (ii) the linear map $V\to V^*$, $u\mapsto g(u,\cdot)$, is an isomorphism; (iii) $\det(g_{ij})\neq 0$ in one, hence every, basis.

for symmetric $g$, sylvester’s law of inertia asserts the existence of a basis in which $g_{ij}$ is diagonal with entries $\pm 1$; the counts $(p,q)$ of $+1$ and $-1$ are independent of basis—the signature $(p,q)$. positive definite means $g(v,v)>0$ for all $v\neq 0$; equivalently signature $(n,0)$. then $\|v\|:=\sqrt{g(v,v)}$ is a norm and $\langle u,v\rangle:=g(u,v)$ is an inner product. kinetic energy $T=\frac12 g_{ij}\dot q^i\dot q^j$ in basics is exactly such a quadratic form built from a positive-definite $g$ in many mechanical models.

gram matrix, independence, and gram–schmidt

given $v_1,\ldots,v_k\in V$ with an inner product $\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle$, their gram matrix is $G\in\mathbb{R}^{k\times k}$ with entries $G_{ab}=\langle v_a,v_b\rangle$. then

$$ \det G \neq 0 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \{v_1,\ldots,v_k\}\ \text{is linearly independent}. $$

gram–schmidt turns an arbitrary independent ordered set into an orthonormal one. set $u_1=v_1/\|v_1\|$. for $k\ge 2$, define the unnormalized residual

$$ \tilde u_k := v_k-\sum_{j=1}^{k-1}\langle v_k,u_j\rangle\,u_j,\qquad u_k:=\tilde u_k/\|\tilde u_k\|. $$

then $\langle u_i,u_j\rangle=\delta_{ij}$ and $\mathrm{span}\{u_1,\ldots,u_k\}=\mathrm{span}\{v_1,\ldots,v_k\}$. this algorithm is the constructive proof that every finite-dimensional inner-product space admits an orthonormal basis—needed whenever one diagonalizes quadratic forms by orthogonal change of basis.

complex inner products and complexification

for $\mathbb{C}$-linear quantum amplitudes one uses a sesquilinear inner product $\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle:V\times V\to\mathbb{C}$, convention here: conjugate linear in the first slot, linear in the second:

$$ \begin{aligned} \langle \alpha u_1+\beta u_2,\,v\rangle &= \bar\alpha\langle u_1,v\rangle+\bar\beta\langle u_2,v\rangle,\\ \langle u,\,\gamma v_1+\delta v_2\rangle &= \gamma\langle u,v_1\rangle+\delta\langle u,v_2\rangle. \end{aligned} $$

hermitian symmetry and positive definiteness are

$$ \langle v,u\rangle=\overline{\langle u,v\rangle},\qquad \langle v,v\rangle>0\ \text{for }v\neq 0. $$

in a basis, $\langle u,v\rangle=\sum_{i,j}\overline{u^i}\,h_{ij}\,v^j$ with $h_{ij}=\overline{h_{ji}}$ hermitian. the complexification of a real vector space $V_{\mathbb{R}}$ is $V_{\mathbb{C}}:=V_{\mathbb{R}}\otimes_{\mathbb{R}}\mathbb{C}$, extending scalars; an $\mathbb{R}$-linear map $J:V_{\mathbb{R}}\to V_{\mathbb{R}}$ with $J^2=-\mathrm{id}$ encodes an alternative complex structure when present.

minkowski inner product, causal type, and lorentz isometries

special relativity fixes a nondegenerate symmetric form of signature $(1,3)$ or $(3,1)$ on $\mathbb{R}^4$. one standard component form (matching $ds^2=c^2dt^2-dx^2-dy^2-dz^2$) is

$$ \eta_{\mu\nu}=\mathrm{diag}(1,-1,-1,-1),\qquad \langle x,y\rangle_\eta := \eta_{\mu\nu}x^\mu y^\nu. $$

the invariant interval along a displacement $dx^\mu$ is

$$ ds^2=\eta_{\mu\nu}\,dx^\mu dx^\nu. $$

a nonzero vector $v^\mu$ is timelike if $\eta_{\mu\nu}v^\mu v^\nu>0$, spacelike if $\eta_{\mu\nu}v^\mu v^\nu<0$, null (lightlike) if $\eta_{\mu\nu}v^\mu v^\nu=0$. the boundary of the timelike cones is the light cone. linear maps $\Lambda:\mathbb{R}^4\to\mathbb{R}^4$ preserving $\eta$ in the sense $\langle \Lambda x,\Lambda y\rangle_\eta=\langle x,y\rangle_\eta$ for all $x,y$ are exactly the lorentz transformations; in matrix form,

$$ \Lambda^{\mathsf T}\eta\,\Lambda=\eta. $$

they form the lie group $O(1,3)$. the indefinite signature is forced if one wants a nondegenerate symmetric pairing and a finite invariant speed of light in the usual spacetime kinematics—see special relativity in basics.

symplectic vector spaces, darboux form, and poisson bracket

a symplectic form on real finite-dimensional $V$ is a bilinear $\omega:V\times V\to\mathbb{R}$ that is skew-symmetric and nondegenerate:

$$ \omega(u,v)=-\omega(v,u),\qquad \bigl(\omega(u,v)=0\ \forall v\bigr)\Rightarrow u=0. $$

then $\dim V=2n$ is even. there exists a darboux basis in which the matrix of $\omega$ is

$$ [\omega_{ij}] = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & I_n \\ -I_n & 0 \end{pmatrix}. $$

writing coordinates as $(q^1,\ldots,q^n,p_1,\ldots,p_n)$ for that basis, the dual pairing is often packaged with the wedge product on $T^*Q$ as $\omega=\sum_{i=1}^n dq^i\wedge dp_i$; the same antisymmetric matrix encodes the bilinear pairing on the $2n$-dimensional model of phase space. the poisson bracket of smooth functions $f,g$ on an open set in those coordinates is

$$ \{f,g\}:=\sum_{i=1}^n\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial q^i}\frac{\partial g}{\partial p_i}-\frac{\partial f}{\partial p_i}\frac{\partial g}{\partial q^i}\right). $$

equivalently, $\{f,g\}=\omega(X_f,X_g)$ where $X_f$ is the hamiltonian vector field defined by $\iota_{X_f}\omega=\mathrm{d}f$. this is the linear infinitesimal backbone of phase space and canonical structure. symplectic geometry answers a different physical demand than a metric: it measures canonical pairing and phase-space volume ($\omega^n/n!$), not lengths of single vectors.

tensor product: universal bilinearity

given finite-dimensional $V,W$ over $\mathbb{R}$ (or $\mathbb{C}$), the tensor product $V\otimes W$ is built so that $(v,w)\mapsto v\otimes w$ is bilinear and every bilinear $B:V\times W\to U$ factors uniquely through a linear $\tilde B:V\otimes W\to U$ with $\tilde B(v\otimes w)=B(v,w)$. the generating relations are

$$ \begin{aligned} (\alpha v_1+\beta v_2)\otimes w &= \alpha(v_1\otimes w)+\beta(v_2\otimes w),\\ v\otimes(\gamma w_1+\delta w_2) &= \gamma(v\otimes w_1)+\delta(v\otimes w_2). \end{aligned} $$

a tensor of type $(p,q)$ on $V$ is an element of $V^{\otimes p}\otimes (V^*)^{\otimes q}$. if $S$ is the change-of-basis matrix on $V$ (new components $v'^i=S^i{}_j v^j$), contravariant components pick up $S$, covariant components pick up $S^{-1}$:

$$ \tilde T^{i_1\cdots i_p}{}_{j_1\cdots j_q} = S^{i_1}{}_{a_1}\cdots S^{i_p}{}_{a_p}\,(S^{-1})^{b_1}{}_{j_1}\cdots(S^{-1})^{b_q}{}_{j_q}\, T^{a_1\cdots a_p}{}_{b_1\cdots b_q}. $$

a nondegenerate inner product $g_{ij}$ induces the musical isomorphisms $\flat:V\to V^*$ and $\sharp:V^*\to V$:

$$ v^\flat(w):=g(v,w),\qquad g(\alpha^\sharp,w):=\alpha(w), $$

and in components $v_i=g_{ij}v^j$, $v^i=g^{ij}v_j$ where $g^{ij}$ is the inverse matrix of $g_{ij}$.

symmetrization, alternation, and the exterior algebra

on covariant rank-$p$ tensors (or components $T_{i_1\ldots i_p}$), the symmetrization and alternation operators are

$$ (\mathrm{Sym}\,T)_{i_1\ldots i_p}:=\frac{1}{p!}\sum_{\sigma\in S_p} T_{i_{\sigma(1)}\ldots i_{\sigma(p)}}, $$ $$ (\mathrm{Alt}\,T)_{i_1\ldots i_p}:=\frac{1}{p!}\sum_{\sigma\in S_p} \mathrm{sgn}(\sigma)\,T_{i_{\sigma(1)}\ldots i_{\sigma(p)}}. $$

the exterior power $\Lambda^k V^*$ consists of alternating multilinear forms; the wedge product may be defined by $\alpha\wedge\beta:=\mathrm{Alt}(\alpha\otimes\beta)$ (up to the conventional factorial normalization). graded commutativity holds:

$$ \alpha\wedge\beta=(-1)^{pq}\,\beta\wedge\alpha,\qquad \alpha\in\Lambda^p,\ \beta\in\Lambda^q. $$

in particular $v\wedge v=0$ for any one-form $v$; iterated wedges yield oriented $k$-volumes and are the algebraic substrate of differential $k$-forms in global forms and in the next section on manifolds.

how these layers attach to the classical spine

positive-definite symmetric $g$ supplies lengths, angles, orthonormal frames, and the quadratic kinetic energy; hermitian inner products supply complex amplitudes with a notion of magnitude. the minkowski form $\eta$ supplies causal order and lorentz symmetry. the symplectic form $\omega$ supplies canonical coordinates, hamiltonian flows, and poisson brackets without choosing a riemannian metric on phase space. tensor products and index bookkeeping encode multilinear physical laws under change of coordinates; the exterior algebra encodes antisymmetric line- and surface-integrand data that glue globally as forms. manifolds, metrics, connections, and cohomology in the sections that follow assume this multilinear layer is already fixed pointwise on tangent spaces.

differentiable manifolds and the calculus of forms

vector spaces, tensors, and the exterior algebra live on a single model space $\mathbb{R}^n$. configuration spaces, spacetimes, and constrained surfaces in basics are not always globally identifiable with one $\mathbb{R}^n$: rigid rotation groups, nontrivial topology, and curvature all obstruct a single global chart. the minimal repair is a topological space that is locally euclidean, together with a consistent $C^\infty$ calculus across overlaps. that is a smooth manifold; on it sit $TM$, $T^*M$, forms, $\mathrm{d}$, and partitions of unity—the language of curved spacetime and fields as forms.

topological manifolds and smooth atlases

a topological $n$-manifold is a hausdorff, second-countable topological space $M$ such that every point admits an open neighborhood homeomorphic to an open subset of $\mathbb{R}^n$. hausdorff and second-countability exclude pathologies (non-separable spaces, non-unique limits) and are part of the standard definition so partitions of unity and embedding theorems apply.

a $C^k$ chart on $M$ is a pair $(U,\varphi)$ with $U\subset M$ open and $\varphi:U\to\varphi(U)\subset\mathbb{R}^n$ a homeomorphism onto an open set. a $C^k$ atlas is a family $\{(U_\alpha,\varphi_\alpha)\}_{\alpha\in A}$ such that $\bigcup_\alpha U_\alpha=M$ and, on every nonempty overlap $U_\alpha\cap U_\beta$, the transition map

$$ \varphi_{\beta\alpha}:=\varphi_\beta\circ\varphi_\alpha^{-1}:\ \varphi_\alpha(U_\alpha\cap U_\beta)\to \varphi_\beta(U_\alpha\cap U_\beta) $$

is of class $C^k$ as a map between open subsets of $\mathbb{R}^n$. two atlases are equivalent if their union is again a $C^k$ atlas. a $C^k$ manifold is $M$ equipped with a maximal atlas (equivalently, an equivalence class). smooth ($C^\infty$) manifolds are the default for field theory; then all transition maps are $C^\infty$, and one may differentiate arbitrarily in coordinates.

the point of the transition condition: a function $f:M\to\mathbb{R}$ is declared smooth if $f\circ\varphi_\alpha^{-1}$ is smooth on $\varphi_\alpha(U_\alpha)$ for every chart. compatibility on overlaps makes this definition independent of which chart contains $p$—the same construction carries tangent vectors, tensors, and forms without coordinate ambiguity.

tangent and cotangent spaces, bundles, frames

fix $p\in M$. a tangent vector at $p$ may be defined either as an equivalence class of smooth curves $\gamma:(-\varepsilon,\varepsilon)\to M$ with $\gamma(0)=p$ (same first derivative in any chart) or as a derivation $D:C^\infty(M)\to\mathbb{R}$ at $p$ satisfying the leibniz rule $D(fg)=f(p)Dg+g(p)Df$. these definitions yield the same finite-dimensional vector space $T_pM$, $\dim T_pM=n$.

the tangent bundle is the disjoint union $TM:=\bigsqcup_{p\in M}T_pM$ with the standard smooth manifold structure of dimension $2n$: local coordinates $(x^i)$ on $U$ induce coordinates $(x^i,v^i)$ on $TU$ representing $v=\sum_i v^i\partial/\partial x^i$. a vector field is a smooth section $X:M\to TM$. the cotangent space $T_p^*M:=(T_pM)^*$ carries covectors; the cotangent bundle $T^*M=\bigsqcup_p T_p^*M$ has dimension $2n$, and a smooth section is a differential one-form $\alpha$ with $\alpha_p\in T_p^*M$. pairing is canonical:

$$ \langle \alpha_p, v\rangle := \alpha_p(v)\in\mathbb{R},\qquad v\in T_pM. $$

momenta and generalized forces in mechanics are naturally covectors; velocities are tangent vectors. the frame bundle $F(M)$ has fiber over $p$ the set of ordered bases (frames) of $T_pM$; the group $GL(n,\mathbb{R})$ acts freely and transitively on each fiber. it is the natural domain for connection forms and, in gauge theory, for local trivializations of linear-algebraic data attached to each point.

partitions of unity and gluing local data

many constructions—riemannian metrics, integrals of densities, bump functions—start per chart. to obtain global objects one uses a partition of unity subordinate to an open cover $\{U_\alpha\}$: a family $\{\psi_\alpha\}$ of smooth functions $M\to[0,\infty)$ such that

  1. $\mathrm{supp}(\psi_\alpha)\subset U_\alpha$ for each $\alpha$;
  2. the family is locally finite: every $p\in M$ has a neighborhood meeting only finitely many $\mathrm{supp}(\psi_\alpha)$;
  3. $\sum_\alpha \psi_\alpha(p)=1$ for all $p\in M$ (the sum is well-defined by local finiteness).

on a smooth manifold (hausdorff, second-countable), such partitions exist for any open cover. bump functions build the $\psi_\alpha$. typical global assembly formulas are

$$ g=\sum_\alpha \psi_\alpha\,g_\alpha,\qquad \int_M \omega = \sum_\alpha \int_{U_\alpha}\psi_\alpha\,\omega, $$

where $g_\alpha$ is a metric tensor defined on $U_\alpha$ and $\omega$ is an $n$-form; each integrand on the right is compactly supported in a chart domain. this is why global statements in basics (total energy, flux through a closed surface, action integrals) can be defined invariantly even when no single chart covers $M$.

differential forms and the exterior derivative

a smooth differential $p$-form $\omega$ on $M$ assigns each $p\in M$ an alternating multilinear map on $T_pM$:

$$ \omega_p:\ (T_pM)^p\to\mathbb{R},\qquad \omega_p(\ldots,v,\ldots,w,\ldots)=-\omega_p(\ldots,w,\ldots,v,\ldots), $$

varying smoothly in $p$. equivalently, $\omega$ is a smooth section of the bundle $\Lambda^p(T^*M)$. in local coordinates $(x^i)$ on $U$,

$$ \omega|_U=\frac{1}{p!}\,\omega_{i_1\ldots i_p}(x)\,dx^{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge dx^{i_p}, $$

with $\omega_{\ldots}$ totally antisymmetric in its indices; the factor $1/p!$ avoids over-counting when indices are summed without ordering.

the exterior derivative $\mathrm{d}:\Omega^p(M)\to\Omega^{p+1}(M)$ is uniquely characterized by: $\mathrm{d}$ is $\mathbb{R}$-linear; $\mathrm{d}(f)=\partial_i f\,dx^i$ on functions; $\mathrm{d}^2=0$; and the graded leibniz rule

$$ \mathrm{d}(\alpha\wedge\beta)=\mathrm{d}\alpha\wedge\beta+(-1)^{\deg\alpha}\,\alpha\wedge\mathrm{d}\beta. $$

in coordinates, on a $p$-form written with ordered indices $i_1<\cdots<i_p$,

$$ \mathrm{d}\omega=\sum_{j=1}^n \frac{\partial\omega_{i_1\ldots i_p}}{\partial x^j}\,dx^j\wedge dx^{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge dx^{i_p}, $$

with the understanding that components are extended by antisymmetry. the identity $\mathrm{d}^2=0$ is the coordinate-free packaging of equality of mixed partial derivatives (schwarz) for $C^2$ coefficients. on $\mathbb{R}^3$, $\mathrm{d}$ unifies gradient, curl, and divergence. closed forms ($\mathrm{d}\omega=0$) need not be globally exact; that gap is measured by de rham cohomology in the next section—already implicit in maxwell and monopole obstructions.

contraction, lie derivative, and frobenius

for a vector field $X$, interior multiplication (contraction) $\iota_X:\Omega^p(M)\to\Omega^{p-1}(M)$ is the $\Omega(M)$-antiderivation determined by $\iota_X f=0$ on functions and $\iota_X(\mathrm{d}f)=X(f)$. cartan’s magic formula defines the lie derivative of forms:

$$ \mathcal{L}_X := \iota_X\circ\mathrm{d}+\mathrm{d}\circ\iota_X. $$

on vector fields, $\mathcal{L}_XY=[X,Y]$, the lie bracket, equivalently the commutator of flows at first order. a distribution is a subbundle $\Delta\subset TM$ of constant rank; it is involutive if $[X,Y]$ is a section of $\Delta$ whenever $X,Y$ are. frobenius’ theorem states: $\Delta$ is tangent to a foliation by immersed submanifolds (integrable) if and only if $\Delta$ is involutive. in mechanics, holonomic constraints define integrable distributions; non-holonomic constraints (rolling without slipping) yield non-involutive ones—finite rotations do not commute, as seen already on $SO(3)$.

example ($SO(3)$). the configuration space of a rigid body orientation is the lie group $SO(3)$, a three-dimensional manifold. left-invariant vector fields correspond to body angular velocity components; their lie brackets reproduce the cross-product algebra of $\mathbb{R}^3$. the span of two independent left-invariant fields is generically not involutive, consistent with non-commutativity of finite rotations and with the distinction between holonomic and non-holonomic rolling constraints in the same geometric language.

in planar problems with harmonic or holomorphic complex coordinates, conformal maps preserve angles; analytic continuation of potentials along homotopic paths agrees (monodromy), so the domain’s topology controls single-valuedness of potentials—another instance where global shape constrains local analytic continuation.

how this section feeds the rest of the hierarchy

charts and transition maps fix what smooth means globally. $TM$ and $T^*M$ carry velocities and covectors (momenta, gradients) invariantly. partitions of unity make metrics, integrals, and bump-supported corrections globally coherent. $p$-forms and $\mathrm{d}$ encode antisymmetric field content and bianchi-type identities; $\mathcal{L}_X$ and $[X,Y]$ encode symmetries and constraint geometry; frobenius classifies foliations and constraint types. cohomology, connections, and curvature in the following sections are built on this calculus; they are the next algebraic-topological layer on the same smooth manifold, not an ad hoc overlay.

cohomological invariants and spectral topology

the preceding section fixed smooth manifolds, $\mathrm{d}^2=0$, and the calculus of forms. a recurring physical pattern is local solvability without global extension: on a small ball, closed forms admit primitives and gauge potentials exist; across all of $M$, those primitives may fail to patch to a single global object. magnetic flux can close ($\mathrm{d}F=0$) yet admit no global $A$ with $F=\mathrm{d}A$; gauge fields trivialize on patches but twist as a bundle so no global section exists. global and topological closure is the physics of that gap. de rham cohomology (and the tools below) measure it in linear, functorial language.

the de rham complex and cohomology

let $M$ be a smooth manifold. write $\Omega^p(M)$ for smooth real $p$-forms. the de rham complex is the cochain complex

$$ 0\to\Omega^0(M)\xrightarrow{\mathrm{d}}\Omega^1(M)\xrightarrow{\mathrm{d}}\cdots\xrightarrow{\mathrm{d}} \Omega^n(M)\to 0,\qquad n=\dim M, $$

with the exterior derivative $\mathrm{d}$ satisfying, for all $\alpha,\beta$ of appropriate degree and $a,b\in\mathbb{R}$:

  1. $\mathrm{d}(a\alpha+b\beta)=a\,\mathrm{d}\alpha+b\,\mathrm{d}\beta$ (linearity);
  2. $\mathrm{d}(\alpha\wedge\beta)=\mathrm{d}\alpha\wedge\beta+(-1)^{\deg\alpha}\alpha\wedge\mathrm{d}\beta$ (graded leibniz);
  3. $\mathrm{d}^2=0$ on every form (nilpotency—equivalently $\mathrm{im}\,\mathrm{d}\subseteq\ker\mathrm{d}$ in each degree).

define the closed and exact forms

$$ Z^p(M):=\{\omega\in\Omega^p(M):\mathrm{d}\omega=0\},\qquad B^p(M):=\mathrm{im}\bigl(\mathrm{d}:\Omega^{p-1}(M)\to\Omega^p(M)\bigr). $$

exact implies closed because $\mathrm{d}^2=0$. the $p$-th de rham cohomology vector space is the quotient

$$ H^p_{\mathrm{dR}}(M):=Z^p(M)/B^p(M). $$

a class $[\omega]\in H^p_{\mathrm{dR}}(M)$ is the equivalence class of $\omega$ modulo adding $\mathrm{d}\eta$. for compact $M$ (or finite-dimensional cohomology), $\dim H^p_{\mathrm{dR}}(M)=b_p$ is the $p$-th betti number—a topological invariant, independent of metric or coordinates. a smooth map $f:M\to N$ pulls back forms and commutes with $\mathrm{d}$, hence induces well-defined linear maps

$$ f^*:H^p_{\mathrm{dR}}(N)\to H^p_{\mathrm{dR}}(M),\qquad f^*[\omega]=[f^*\omega], $$

functorially: $(g\circ f)^*=f^*\circ g^*$. poincaré lemma: on a contractible open subset of $M$, every closed $p$-form is exact. global topology is exactly what can block the extension of such local primitives.

reading degrees for fields. $H^1_{\mathrm{dR}}(M)\neq 0$ means there are closed $1$-forms not globally $\mathrm{d}f$—multi-valued potentials, holonomy, and aharonov–bohm-type phases live in this language. $H^2_{\mathrm{dR}}(M)\neq 0$ means closed $2$-forms need not be globally $\mathrm{d}A$; that is the cohomological obstruction to a single-valued vector potential when flux or bundle twisting is present.

dirac monopole: closed flux, no global potential

place a static magnetic monopole of strength $g$ at the origin in $\mathbb{R}^3$. on $\mathbb{R}^3\setminus\{0\}$, use standard spherical coordinates $(r,\theta,\phi)$. the magnetic field $2$-form (oriented so flux through a surrounding sphere is $4\pi g$) may be written

$$ F = g\,\sin\theta\,\mathrm{d}\theta\wedge\mathrm{d}\phi. $$

then $\mathrm{d}F=0$ on $\mathbb{R}^3\setminus\{0\}$ (direct computation in these coordinates gives $\mathrm{d}F=0$). the integral over any sphere $S^2$ enclosing the origin is

$$ \int_{S^2} F = \int_0^{2\pi}\!\!\int_0^{\pi} g\sin\theta\,\mathrm{d}\theta\,\mathrm{d}\phi = 4\pi g. $$

$H^2_{\mathrm{dR}}(\mathbb{R}^3\setminus\{0\})\cong H^2_{\mathrm{dR}}(S^2)\cong\mathbb{R}$, and integration over an enclosing $S^2$ identifies a generator: a class is zero if and only if its integral vanishes. thus $g\neq 0$ gives $[F]\neq 0$, so $F$ cannot be exact on the punctured space—there is no smooth global $A$ with $F=\mathrm{d}A$ on all of $\mathbb{R}^3\setminus\{0\}$. local potentials $A_U$ exist on charts (e.g. two hemispheres), but gluing them smoothly forces a dirac string. the cohomology class is the invariant record of that obstruction. coupling to a quantum phase $\exp(\frac{\mathrm{i}e}{\hbar}\oint A)$ along loops then yields dirac-type quantization relations between electric charge $e$ and magnetic charge $g$ (exact normalization depends on units).

mayer–vietoris

to compute $H^*_{\mathrm{dR}}(M)$ from simpler pieces, let $M=U\cup V$ with $U,V$ open. restriction of forms gives a short complex; the resulting mayer–vietoris long exact sequence links cohomology of $M$, $U$, $V$, and $U\cap V$:

$$ \cdots\to H^p_{\mathrm{dR}}(M)\xrightarrow{(j_U^*,j_V^*)} H^p_{\mathrm{dR}}(U)\oplus H^p_{\mathrm{dR}}(V) \xrightarrow{j_{U\cap V,U}^*-j_{U\cap V,V}^*} H^p_{\mathrm{dR}}(U\cap V)\xrightarrow{\delta} H^{p+1}_{\mathrm{dR}}(M)\to\cdots $$

the connecting homomorphism $\delta$ is built by choosing a partition of unity subordinate to $\{U,V\}$, splitting a closed form on $U\cap V$ into pieces extendable to $U$ and $V$, and taking $\mathrm{d}$ of the glued difference—so the same analytic tools that globalize metrics also build topological invariants. iterating on complicated $M$ reduces global cohomology to balls and intersections.

čech–de rham and the de rham theorem

for a good cover $\{U_\alpha\}$ (all finite intersections contractible), one forms a double complex: čech $q$-cochains with values in $p$-forms on $(q+1)$-fold intersections, with čech coboundary $\check{\delta}$ and exterior derivative $\mathrm{d}$ (extended row-wise). signs are arranged so the total differential $D=\mathrm{d}+(-1)^p\check{\delta}$ satisfies $D^2=0$. the associated spectral sequence (or direct acyclic-resolution argument) computes $H^*_{\mathrm{dR}}(M)$ and proves the de rham theorem: $H^p_{\mathrm{dR}}(M)$ is naturally isomorphic to the real čech cohomology $\check{H}^p(M;\mathbb{R})$ of the nerve of the cover. analytic forms and combinatorial topology are the same invariant; that isomorphism is why concrete flux integrals count betti numbers.

poincaré duality

let $M$ be a closed oriented smooth $n$-manifold (compact, no boundary). cup product and integration define a bilinear pairing

$$ H^p_{\mathrm{dR}}(M)\times H^{n-p}_{\mathrm{dR}}(M)\to\mathbb{R},\qquad ([\alpha],[\beta])\mapsto \int_M \alpha\wedge\beta, $$

well-defined because changing $\alpha$ by $\mathrm{d}\eta$ shifts the integrand by an exact $n$-form, which integrates to zero by stokes. this pairing is nondegenerate (poincaré duality), hence $\dim H^p_{\mathrm{dR}}(M)=\dim H^{n-p}_{\mathrm{dR}}(M)$. on noncompact $M$, one often pairs $H^p(M)$ with $H^{n-p}_c(M)$ (compact support on one factor) so integrals converge. geometrically, $p$-cycles (sources, vortices, branes) sit in homology dual to $(n-p)$-cohomology; in four dimensions this is the structural reason electric and magnetic data can be exchanged while preserving global consistency of flux and charge.

thom class, euler class, and nowhere-vanishing sections

let $\pi:E\to M$ be an oriented real vector bundle of rank $k$. the thom class $\Phi_E\in H^k_c(E)$ (compact support in the fiber direction) is characterized by integrating to $1$ along each oriented fiber, generating $H^k(\mathbb{R}^k,\mathbb{R}^k\setminus\{0\})$ fiberwise. the euler class is the pullback along the zero section:

$$ e(E):=s_0^*\,\Phi_E\in H^k_{\mathrm{dR}}(M). $$

obstruction theory: a nowhere-vanishing global section forces $e(E)=0$ (pull the bundle off the zero section). conversely, $e(E)\neq 0$ obstructs every attempt at a continuous nowhere-vanishing section. for an oriented surface $M$, $\int_M e(TM)=\chi(M)$ (gauss–bonnet); in particular $\int_{S^2}e(TS^2)=\chi(S^2)=2\neq 0$, so $e(TS^2)\neq 0$ and no continuous nonvanishing vector field exists on $S^2$ (hairy ball theorem). euler class is multiplicative under whitney sum of oriented bundles:

$$ e(E\oplus F)=e(E)\smile e(F), $$

with $\smile$ the cup product on $H^*(M)$. that multiplicativity underlies product-manifold index calculations and many anomaly-cancellation identities in gauge theory.

spectral sequences and fibrations

a filtered cochain complex $(K^\bullet,d)$ with subcomplexes $F^p K^\bullet$ yields a spectral sequence $\{E_r^{p,q},d_r\}$ with $d_r:E_r^{p,q}\to E_r^{p+r,\,q-r+1}$ and $d_r^2=0$; successive pages approximate $H^{p+q}(K^\bullet)$. for a fibration $F\to E\to B$ with $B$ path-connected and $\pi_1(B)$ acting trivially on $H^*(F)$, the serre spectral sequence has

$$ E_2^{p,q}=H^p_{\mathrm{dR}}\bigl(B;\,H^q_{\mathrm{dR}}(F)\bigr)\quad\Rightarrow\quad H^{p+q}_{\mathrm{dR}}(E). $$

differentials transgress fiber classes into base cohomology, packaging how fiber topology twists the total space. the same formalism organizes čech–de rham doubles, equivariant cohomology, and filtered de rham complexes on flag manifolds and loop spaces. for a map $f:S^{2n-1}\to S^n$ of hopf type, pulling back a volume form and choosing primitives encodes the hopf invariant in integrals such as $\int \alpha\wedge\mathrm{d}\alpha$—linking homotopy of maps to cohomological pairings.

how this feeds what follows

closed versus exact is the differential shadow of global topology; $H^p_{\mathrm{dR}}$ is the linear measurement of that gap. mayer–vietoris and čech–de rham compute it; poincaré duality aligns degrees that carry dual flux and charge; euler and thom classes obstruct sections of bundles on which fields live; spectral sequences compute cohomology of total spaces when base and fiber are understood. the variational and field equations in basics assume this layer when asserting which potentials exist globally, which charges are quantized, and how connections on bundles patch— connections, curvature, and characteristic classes in later sections are built on the same cohomological skeleton.

the kinematics and dynamics of classical mechanics

this section names the geometric objects that the classical narrative in basics manipulates: configuration manifolds, lagrangians on the tangent bundle, legendre transforms to the cotangent bundle, and symplectic hamiltonian flows.

configuration space and the tangent bundle

let \(Q\) be a smooth manifold of generalized coordinates \(q^i\) (possibly with constraints already embedded, so \(Q\) is the reduced configuration space). the tangent bundle \(TQ\) has points \((q,v)\) with \(v\in T_qQ\). a lagrangian is a function \(L:TQ\to\mathbb{R}\) (possibly time dependent); the action on a path \(q(t)\) is \(S[q]=\int L(q,\dot{q},t)\,dt\). stationarity of \(S\) under fixed-endpoint variations yields the euler–lagrange equations in coordinates: \(\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial\dot{q}^i}-\frac{\partial L}{\partial q^i}=0\). this is the coordinate-free content of variational calculus, expressed invariantly as an equation on \(TQ\) or, via the variational principle, on the path space of maps into \(Q\).

legendre transform and the symplectic cotangent bundle

when the fiber hessian \(\partial^2 L/\partial\dot{q}^i\partial\dot{q}^j\) is nondegenerate, the legendre map \(\mathbb{F}L:TQ\to T^*Q\) sends \((q,\dot{q})\mapsto(q,p)\) with \(p_i=\partial L/\partial\dot{q}^i\). the hamiltonian on \(T^*Q\) is \(H=p_i\dot{q}^i-L\) expressed as a function of \((q,p)\). the canonical one-form \(\theta=p_i\,dq^i\) on \(T^*Q\) and the symplectic form \(\omega=-\mathrm{d}\theta=\sum_i dq^i\wedge dp_i\) (in darboux coordinates) make \((T^*Q,\omega)\) a symplectic manifold. hamilton’s equations are the flow of the vector field \(X_H\) defined by \(\iota_{X_H}\omega=\mathrm{d}H\). equivalently, the poisson bracket \(\{f,g\}=\omega(X_f,X_g)\) encodes the same dynamics. this is the geometric content of phase space and canonical structure, linked also to symplectic linear algebra above.

symmetries, constants, integrable structure

a lie group action on \(Q\) or \(TQ\) leaving \(L\) invariant generates conserved quantities via noether’s theorem (made precise via momentum maps in symplectic geometry). hamilton–jacobi theory seeks a generating function \(S(q,t)\) solving \(H(q,\partial S/\partial q)+\partial S/\partial t=0\), rebuilding families of trajectories from a single scalar. in integrable systems, action–angle coordinates linearize flow on invariant tori; adiabatic invariants track approximate conservation of actions under slow parameter change—topics treated concretely in the same classical sections. global issues (e.g. monodromy, nontrivial \(TQ\) or \(T^*Q\) topology) refine the picture but the local hamiltonian formalism above is the universal core.

relativistic spacetime and electrodynamics

the classical development in special relativity, waves and radiation, and maxwell as forms assumes a lorentzian background and gauge fields as connections; here is the compact mathematical packaging.

minkowski space and the lorentz group

choose units with \(c=1\). minkowski space is \(\mathbb{R}^4\) with metric \(\eta=\mathrm{diag} (-1,1,1,1)\) and line element \(ds^2=\eta_{\mu\nu}\,dx^\mu dx^\nu\). a linear map \(\Lambda\) is a lorentz transformation if \(\Lambda^T\eta\Lambda=\eta\). they form the indefinite orthogonal group \(O(1,3)\); the component preserving orientation and time orientation is often written \(SO^+(1,3)\). four-vectors \(V^\mu\) transform by \(V'^\mu=\Lambda^\mu{}_\nu V^\nu\); covectors use \(\eta_{\mu\nu}\) to lower indices. causal structure: vectors with \(\eta(V,V)<0\) are timelike; null vectors define light cones.

maxwell as gauge theory on a lorentzian manifold

on a general lorentzian four-manifold \((M,g)\), the electromagnetic potential is a one-form \(A\); the field strength is the exact two-form \(F=\mathrm{d}A\) (so \(\mathrm{d}F=0\) is the homogeneous maxwell system, bianchi identity). sources enter through the hodge star \(\star_g\) built from \(g\): the inhomogeneous equation is \(\mathrm{d}\star_g F=\star_g J\) for current three-form \(J\). on minkowski space in cartesian coordinates this reproduces the standard \(\partial_\mu F^{\mu\nu}=J^\nu\) after identifying components. gauge freedom \(A\mapsto A+\mathrm{d}\chi\) leaves \(F\) unchanged—precisely a one-form worth of redundancy quotiented in the classical discussion of potentials.

the stress–energy tensor \(T^{\mu\nu}\) packages local energy, momentum, and stress; its divergence vanishes when fields obey the coupled equations of motion (matter plus maxwell), encoding conservation compatible with lorentz symmetry. retarded potentials and liénard–wiechert fields are explicit green-function solutions tied to causal support of sources on lorentzian manifolds—the same causality enforced geometrically by the metric cone structure.

geometric gravity, connections, and curvature

general relativity and curved spacetime identifies the gravitational field with a lorentzian metric \(g\) on a four-manifold \(M\). the mathematical layer spells out how \(g\) fixes geometry, curvature, and field equations.

levi-civita connection and curvature

a metric on \(M\) is a smooth symmetric nondegenerate two-tensor \(g\) of lorentzian signature. the levi-civita connection \(\nabla\) is the unique affine connection that is metric compatible (\(\nabla g=0\)) and torsion-free. in coordinates, christoffel symbols \(\Gamma^k_{ij}=\tfrac12 g^{k\ell}(\partial_i g_{j\ell}+\partial_j g_{i\ell}-\partial_\ell g_{ij})\) define covariant derivatives \(\nabla_i V^k=\partial_i V^k+\Gamma^k_{ij}V^j\).

the riemann curvature tensor \(R^\rho{}_{\sigma\mu\nu}\) measures failure of covariant derivatives to commute and encodes holonomy of parallel transport around infinitesimal loops. the ricci tensor is the contraction \(R_{\mu\nu}=R^\rho{}_{\mu\rho\nu}\); the ricci scalar is \(R=g^{\mu\nu}R_{\mu\nu}\). the einstein tensor \(G_{\mu\nu}=R_{\mu\nu}-\tfrac12 R\,g_{\mu\nu}\) satisfies the contracted bianchi identity \(\nabla_\mu G^{\mu\nu}=0\), parallel to conservation of stress–energy.

einstein’s equations and vacuum solutions

the einstein–hilbert action (with cosmological constant \(\Lambda\) optional) is \(S=\frac{1}{16\pi G}\int_M (R-2\Lambda)\,\mathrm{vol}_g+\int_M \mathcal{L}_m\,\mathrm{vol}_g\). variation with respect to \(g\) yields einstein’s equations \(G_{\mu\nu}+\Lambda g_{\mu\nu}=8\pi G\,T_{\mu\nu}\), where \(T_{\mu\nu}\) is the matter stress–energy tensor. vacuum solutions (\(T_{\mu\nu}=0\)) include schwarzschild, the unique static, spherically symmetric, asymptotically flat black hole geometry in four dimensions (birkhoff’s theorem). geodesics of \(g\) are the worldlines of freely falling test particles; deviation of nearby geodesics is controlled by the riemann tensor—source of tidal forces and, in the weak-field limit, the extra \(r^{-3}\) contribution to perihelion precession described in basics.
