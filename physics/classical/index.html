symmetry, composition, variation

this block is written to be self-contained and to follow the logical order fixed in CLASSICAL_PHYSICS_AND_MATHEMATICS_UNIFIED.md (§3, symmetry–composition–variation, items A1–A4 there): algebraic composition laws for redundancies, stationary action and euler–lagrange equations, linearized dynamics and spectral data, then categorical bookkeeping for changes of description. nothing below points outward to other site pages; the only external pointer is that syllabus file, for parity of scope.

the motivating tension is plain: experiments can be translated, rotated, or time-shifted without changing outcome laws, yet a single trajectory is realized. classical mechanics resolves this by (i) encoding redundancies as a compositional algebra acting on configuration data, (ii) selecting motion by a scalar functional whose first variation vanishes, (iii) analyzing small departures from equilibrium by linear operators whose spectral invariants carry stability, and (iv) requiring that re-coordinatizations and reductions be morphisms—or functors—not ad hoc renamings.

algebraic structure of symmetries

definition (monoid). a monoid is a set $M$ with a binary operation $\cdot : M\times M\to M$ satisfying

  1. M1 (associativity). $(a\cdot b)\cdot c = a\cdot(b\cdot c)$ for all $a,b,c\in M$.
  2. M2 (identity). there exists $e\in M$ such that $e\cdot a = a\cdot e = a$ for all $a\in M$ (such an $e$ is unique: if $e'$ is another identity, $e = e\cdot e' = e'$).

definition (group). a group is a set $G$ with $\cdot : G\times G\to G$ satisfying

  1. G1 (associativity). $(a\cdot b)\cdot c = a\cdot(b\cdot c)$.
  2. G2 (identity). $\exists\, e\in G$ with $e\cdot a = a\cdot e = a$ for all $a$.
  3. G3 (inverses). $\forall\, a\in G$, $\exists\, a^{-1}\in G$ with $a\cdot a^{-1} = a^{-1}\cdot a = e$.

in reversible conservative mechanics the relevant symmetries are modeled as groups: every allowed transformation should be undoable along solutions. coarse-grained or dissipative models often retain only a monoid of “forward-evolution” morphisms.

definition (left group action). an action of $G$ on a set $Q$ is a map $G\times Q\to Q$, written $(g,q)\mapsto g\cdot q$, such that

$$ e\cdot q = q,\qquad (g\cdot h)\cdot q = g\cdot(h\cdot q)\quad\text{for all }g,h\in G,\; q\in Q. $$

the map $\rho:G\to \mathrm{Sym}(Q)$ given by $\rho(g)(q)=g\cdot q$ is a homomorphism into the group of bijections of $Q$, and conversely any such homomorphism defines an action. a symmetry of a lagrangian system (global, classical) is usually taken to be data $(G,Q,\cdot)$ together with an action on the tangent-level variables $(q,\dot q)$—often induced by an action on $Q$—such that $L$ is invariant, or changes at most by a total time derivative:

$$ L(g\cdot q, g\cdot \dot q, t) = L(q,\dot q,t) + \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t}\Lambda_g(q,t) $$

for some function $\Lambda_g$ on the trajectory (then $\delta S$ for fixed-endpoint variations is unchanged, so the same EL equations result). the precise infinitesimal version (noether currents) belongs to the geometric-mechanics arc; here the point is only that composition of such transformations is modeled by multiplication in $G$.

definition (homomorphism). if $(G,\cdot_G)$ and $(H,\cdot_H)$ are groups, a map $\varphi:G\to H$ is a homomorphism iff

$$ \varphi(g_1\cdot_G g_2) = \varphi(g_1)\cdot_H \varphi(g_2)\quad\text{for all }g_1,g_2\in G. $$

necessarily $\varphi(e_G)=e_H$ and $\varphi(g^{-1})=\varphi(g)^{-1}$. changing coordinates or units should intertwine actions by a homomorphism; otherwise “composition of changes of chart” does not match group multiplication in a fixed reference model.

examples (matrix groups acting on $\mathbb{R}^n$). the orthogonal group is

$$ O(n) = \{\, R\in \mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{R}) : R^{\mathsf T}R = I_n \,\},\qquad SO(n) = \{\, R\in O(n) : \det R = 1 \,\}. $$

each $R\in O(n)$ acts on $x\in\mathbb{R}^n$ by $x\mapsto Rx$, preserving euclidean length $\lVert x\rVert^2 = x^{\mathsf T}x$. translations form the additive group $(\mathbb{R}^n,+)$ acting by $x\mapsto x+a$. the euclidean group $E(n)$ is a semidirect product $\mathbb{R}^n\rtimes O(n)$ encoding rotations/reflections together with translations; galilean and poincaré groups enlarge this picture with boosts and time translations (developed later on this page’s progression).

remark (what is being composed). elements of $G$ may be realized as active maps on $Q$, as passive coordinate relabelings, or as automorphisms of an algebraic model; in each case the same associativity and identity axioms prevent ambiguous iteration of “symmetry after symmetry.”

action, variation, euler–lagrange

fix an open region of configuration space with coordinates $q=(q^1,\ldots,q^n)$ and consider $C^1$ trajectories $q:[t_1,t_2]\to\mathbb{R}^n$. given a lagrangian $L(q,\dot q,t)$, the action functional is

$$ S[q] = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} L\bigl(q(t),\dot q(t),t\bigr)\,\mathrm{d}t . $$

principle of stationary action. physical trajectories are critical points of $S$ in the class of variations $q\mapsto q+\delta q$ with fixed endpoints $\delta q(t_1)=\delta q(t_2)=0$, where $\delta q$ is $C^1$.

lemma (commutation of $\delta$ and $\mathrm{d}/\mathrm{d}t$). for such variations, $\delta\dot q^i = \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t}(\delta q^i)$.

computation of $\delta S$. to first order in $\delta q$,

$$ \delta S = \int_{t_1}^{t_2}\left( \frac{\partial L}{\partial q^i}\,\delta q^i + \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q^i}\,\delta\dot q^i \right)\mathrm{d}t = \int_{t_1}^{t_2}\left( \frac{\partial L}{\partial q^i}\,\delta q^i + \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q^i}\,\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t}(\delta q^i) \right)\mathrm{d}t . $$

integrate the second term by parts:

$$ \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q^i}\,\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t}(\delta q^i)\,\mathrm{d}t = \left[\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q^i}\,\delta q^i\right]_{t_1}^{t_2} - \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t}\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q^i}\right)\delta q^i\,\mathrm{d}t . $$

the boundary bracket vanishes by $\delta q(t_1)=\delta q(t_2)=0$. collecting coefficients of the independent $\delta q^i(t)$ on $(t_1,t_2)$ gives

$$ \delta S = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \left( \frac{\partial L}{\partial q^i} - \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q^i} \right)\delta q^i\,\mathrm{d}t . $$

since the $\delta q^i$ are arbitrary inside the interval, $\delta S=0$ for all admissible variations if and only if the euler–lagrange equations hold:

$$ \boxed{\; \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q^i} - \frac{\partial L}{\partial q^i} = 0, \qquad i=1,\ldots,n . \;} $$

conjugate momenta and hamiltonian. define $p_i = \partial L/\partial\dot q^i$. when the legendre transform is well defined (hessian matrix $\partial^2 L/\partial\dot q^i\,\partial\dot q^j$ invertible), set

$$ H(q,p,t) = \sum_{i=1}^n p_i\,\dot q^i - L(q,\dot q,t), $$

expressing $\dot q$ implicitly as a function of $(q,p,t)$.

proposition (energy balance). along solutions of EL, if $H$ is defined as above,

$$ \frac{\mathrm{d}H}{\mathrm{d}t} = -\,\frac{\partial L}{\partial t}. $$

proof. along a trajectory, using EL and the chain rule,

$$ \frac{\mathrm{d}H}{\mathrm{d}t} = \sum_i \left(\dot p_i\,\dot q^i + p_i\,\ddot q^i\right) - \frac{\mathrm{d}L}{\mathrm{d}t}, \qquad \frac{\mathrm{d}L}{\mathrm{d}t} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial q^i}\dot q^i + \frac{\partial L}{\partial\dot q^i}\ddot q^i + \frac{\partial L}{\partial t}. $$

since $\dot p_i = \mathrm{d}/\mathrm{d}t(\partial L/\partial\dot q^i) = \partial L/\partial q^i$, the terms $\dot p_i\dot q^i$ and $p_i\ddot q^i$ cancel against those from $\mathrm{d}L/\mathrm{d}t$, leaving $\mathrm{d}H/\mathrm{d}t = -\partial L/\partial t$. in particular, if $L$ has no explicit time dependence, $H$ is conserved on solutions.

proposition (gauge equivalence). if $L'(q,\dot q,t) = L(q,\dot q,t) + \mathrm{d}f/\mathrm{d}t$ for a $C^1$ function $f(q,t)$, then $S$ and $S'$ differ only by an endpoint term $f(q(t_2),t_2)-f(q(t_1),t_1)$, which is unchanged under fixed-endpoint variations of $q$. hence $L$ and $L'$ share the same EL equations.

worked example (harmonic oscillator). take $n=1$,

$$ L(x,\dot x) = \frac{m}{2}\dot x^2 - \frac{k}{2}x^2 . $$

then $\partial L/\partial x = -kx$, $\partial L/\partial\dot x = m\dot x$, and EL gives $m\ddot x + kx = 0$. here $p=m\dot x$ and $H = p^2/(2m) + kx^2/2$ is constant on solutions.

remark (coordinate covariance, preview). under a time-independent invertible change $q\mapsto \tilde q(q)$, the velocity transforms linearly, $\dot{\tilde q}^j = (\partial \tilde q^j/\partial q^i)\dot q^i$. requiring $L$ to be a scalar (same number in every coordinate system) forces the EL expressions to transform as components of a covector along the path; the intrinsic geometric statement waits until configuration space is treated as a manifold elsewhere in this progression.

superposition, modes, stability

vector space axioms (real coefficients). a real vector space is a set $V$ with addition $+:V\times V\to V$ and scalar multiplication $\mathbb{R}\times V\to V$ satisfying closure, associativity and commutativity of $+$, existence of $0$ and additive inverses, distributivity, and $1\cdot v = v$. small displacements $\eta$ from an equilibrium live in $V=\mathbb{R}^n$ once coordinates are fixed.

linearization from a lagrangian. let $q_0$ be an equilibrium: the generalized force $-\partial V/\partial q^i$ vanishes at $q_0$ for a natural system, or more generally the EL equations admit constant solution $q(t)\equiv q_0$. write $q = q_0 + \eta$ and expand $L$ to quadratic order in $(\eta,\dot\eta)$ around $(0,0)$. in the standard case where the kinetic term is already quadratic in velocities at $q_0$ and the mixed $\partial^2 L/\partial q^i\partial\dot q^j$ vanishes at equilibrium, one obtains matrices

$$ M_{ij} = \left.\frac{\partial^2 L}{\partial\dot q^i\,\partial\dot q^j}\right|_{q_0},\qquad K_{ij} = \left.\frac{\partial^2 L}{\partial q^i\,\partial q^j}\right|_{q_0} $$

(sign conventions vary with how potential terms are packaged; the mechanical normal form used here is $M\ddot\eta + K\eta = 0$ with $M$ symmetric positive definite and $K$ symmetric for natural systems expanded from $L=T-V$.) EL linearize to

$$ \boxed{\; M\ddot\eta + K\eta = 0 \;} $$

with $M,K\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$ symmetric and $M$ positive definite in the generic mechanical setting.

normal modes. seek time-harmonic solutions $\eta(t) = u\,\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\omega t}$ with $u\in\mathbb{C}^n$. then $(K-\omega^2 M)u = 0$, so nontrivial $u$ exist iff

$$ \det(K-\omega^2 M) = 0 . $$

the roots $\omega_k^2$ are real because $M$ defines an inner product $\langle u,v\rangle_M = u^{\mathsf T}M v$ with respect to which $M^{-1}K$ is self-adjoint on $\mathbb{R}^n$; there exists an $M$-orthonormal basis of real mode shapes $u_k$ with

$$ u_j^{\mathsf T} M u_k = \delta_{jk},\qquad u_j^{\mathsf T} K u_k = \omega_k^2\,\delta_{jk}. $$

expanding $\eta(t) = \sum_k a_k u_k\cos(\omega_k t + \phi_k)$ (for $\omega_k^2>0$) exhibits superposition of decoupled harmonic modes.

stability criteria. if all $\omega_k^2>0$, small perturbations remain bounded (elliptic equilibrium). if some $\omega_k^2<0$, there are real exponential modes $\mathrm{e}^{\pm\lvert\omega_k\rvert t}$ and the equilibrium is linearly unstable. zero eigenvalues indicate neutral directions (often tied to continuous symmetry); their nonlinear fate requires reduced coordinates or constraint analysis.

defective case (jordan form). for a first-order system $\dot x = Ax$ with $A\in\mathbb{R}^{m\times m}$ not diagonalizable, the jordan normal form exhibits blocks

$$ J = \lambda I + N,\qquad N^r=0\quad(N\ \text{strictly upper triangular}), $$

producing solutions with polynomial prefactors $t^k\mathrm{e}^{\lambda t}$. concretely, for

$$ A = \begin{pmatrix} \lambda & 1 \\ 0 & \lambda \end{pmatrix}, $$

the system $\dot x_1 = \lambda x_1 + x_2$, $\dot x_2=\lambda x_2$ integrates to

$$ x_2(t) = c_2\,\mathrm{e}^{\lambda t},\qquad x_1(t) = \bigl(c_1 + c_2 t\bigr)\,\mathrm{e}^{\lambda t}. $$

a second-order conservative system with symmetric $M,K$ cannot produce nontrivial jordan blocks for $M^{-1}K$ over $\mathbb{C}$ (self-adjoint operators diagonalize); defective structure appears for nonsymmetric linearizations (gyroscopic or dissipative couplings) or non-mechanical linearizations. the correct normal form is then jordan, not orthogonal modes.

two-degree-of-freedom spring example. masses $m_1,m_2$ at displacements $x_1,x_2$ from equilibrium, springs $k_1,k_2,k_3$ connecting wall–$m_1$–$m_2$–wall, gives

$$ M = \begin{pmatrix} m_1 & 0 \\ 0 & m_2 \end{pmatrix},\qquad K = \begin{pmatrix} k_1+k_2 & -k_2 \\ -k_2 & k_2+k_3 \end{pmatrix}, $$

and $M\ddot\eta + K\eta=0$. solving $\det(K-\omega^2 M)=0$ yields two positive $\omega^2$ values (for positive-definite $K$), i.e. two physical mode frequencies; mode vectors are found by substituting each $\omega^2$ back into $(K-\omega^2 M)u=0$.

bookkeeping across descriptions

one physical system admits many faithful mathematical models: cartesian versus center-of-mass coordinates, body-fixed versus space-fixed frames, fourier coefficients of fields. the requirement is that structure-preserving comparison maps compose cleanly.

definition (category, axioms). a category $\mathcal{C}$ consists of a class $\mathrm{Ob}(\mathcal{C})$ of objects, for each pair $A,B$ a set $\mathrm{Hom}(A,B)$ of morphisms, composition $\circ:\mathrm{Hom}(B,C)\times\mathrm{Hom}(A,B)\to\mathrm{Hom}(A,C)$, and identity morphisms $1_A\in\mathrm{Hom}(A,A)$, such that

  1. C1 (associativity). $(h\circ g)\circ f = h\circ(g\circ f)$ whenever composites are defined.
  2. C2 (units). $1_B\circ f = f$ and $g\circ 1_B = g$ for $f:A\to B$, $g:B\to C$.

definition (functor). a (covariant) functor $F:\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{D}$ assigns $F(A)\in\mathrm{Ob}(\mathcal{D})$ to each object and $F(f):F(A)\to F(B)$ to each $f:A\to B$, obeying

$$ F(g\circ f) = F(g)\circ F(f),\qquad F(1_A) = 1_{F(A)}. $$

linearization at a chosen equilibrium is a functorial passage from a nonlinear category of pointed mechanical systems to the category of vector spaces and linear maps; forgetting extra structure (e.g. riemannian metric on $Q$) is another functor.

universal property (product, diagrammatic uniqueness). the product $A\times B$ in a category, when it exists, is an object with projections $\pi_A:A\times B\to A$ and $\pi_B:A\times B\to B$ such that for any object $X$ and morphisms $f:X\to A$, $g:X\to B$ there is a unique morphism $(f,g):X\to A\times B$ with $\pi_A\circ(f,g)=f$ and $\pi_B\circ(f,g)=g$. this pins down $A\times B$ up to unique isomorphism and is the correct abstraction of “independent joint degrees of freedom.”

quotient by group action. if $G$ acts on $Q$ and observables are constant on orbits, reduced states live in the orbit space $Q/G$. when the action is free and proper (smooth case), $Q/G$ is a smooth manifold and reduction is functorial at the level of appropriate categories of manifolds with group action. singular strata appear when stabilizers vary—then bookkeeping uses stratified geometry rather than a single manifold.

normal subgroup and residual group. if $H\triangleleft G$ is normal ($gHg^{-1}=H$ for all $g\in G$), the quotient $G/H$ inherits a group structure from coset multiplication. physically, $H$ might be internal gauge or forgotten bookkeeping symmetry, and $G/H$ encodes residual rigid motions.

short exact sequence. a sequence of group homomorphisms

$$ 1 \longrightarrow A \xrightarrow{\ \iota\ } B \xrightarrow{\ \pi\ } C \longrightarrow 1 $$

is exact if $\iota$ is injective, $\pi$ is surjective, and $\mathrm{im}\,\iota = \ker\pi$. then every $c\in C$ admits a lift $\tilde c\in B$ with $\pi(\tilde c)=c$, and two lifts differ by an element of $A$. the same logical pattern governs extensions in linear algebra and, later, closed-versus-exact forms in de rham cohomology: obstructions are classes of ambiguities, not ad hoc choices.
