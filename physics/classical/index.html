symmetry, composition, variation

arc. postulate homogeneity and isotropy of space and time, then ask what dynamics is compatible. the variational principle is the hinge; linear algebra is the language of small disturbances; categories are honest bookkeeping when the same system is described many ways.

algebraic structure of symmetries

p. why should conserved quantities accompany continuous symmetries? because redundant directions in configuration space imply constraints on allowed motion—noether’s story begins here.

m. a symmetry group: composition, identity, inverses; morphisms that preserve structure. start from monoids if you want to expose irreversibility before groups.

questions. what is being composed (states? maps? coordinate changes)? what must be invertible for a “symmetry” of equations?

outcomes. state the group axioms; recognize $O(3)$, translations, and (later) $SO^+(1,3)$ as symmetry groups in concrete models.

action, variation, euler–lagrange

p. the action $S=\int L\,\mathrm{d}t$ is stationary on physical paths with fixed endpoints; EL equations are the necessary conditions.

m. variations $\delta q$ as tangent directions; integration by parts; the EL expression as a covector along the trajectory (coordinate independence comes fully with manifolds).

questions. why fixed endpoints? what if $L$ depends explicitly on time?

outcomes. derive EL in coordinates; compute conjugate momenta $\partial L/\partial\dot q$; recover galilean kinetic energy from symmetry arguments.

multilinear pairings

arc. not every physical “length” is positive-definite; not every pairing is an inner product. kinetic energy wants $+$ definite quadratic forms; spacetime intervals want lorentzian signature; hamiltonian mechanics wants symplectic pairing. tensors and wedges package multilinearity and antisymmetry for fields and fluxes.

m. symmetric bilinear forms; gram matrix; nondegeneracy; polarization identity.

outcomes. read off $g_{ij}$ from $T$; test nondegeneracy; relate to mass matrix in normal-mode problems.
